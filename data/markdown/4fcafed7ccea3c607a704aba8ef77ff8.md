A holistic reading of the Appendix forming 
part of the JSR 2001 leaves no manner of doubt that the 
competitive examination for appointment as Civil


# Page 10

10
Judge-cum-Judicial Magistrate is conceived as a 
composite 
and 
multi-stage 
assessment process, 
consisting not only of written papers but also of viva 
voce, 
each 
carrying 
independent 
qualifying 
requirements. The Appendix expressly prescribes the 
subjects, marks allocation, qualifying criteria and 
method of determining merit, and categorically 
provides that no candidate shall be summoned for viva 
voce unless he or she secures at least 33% marks in each 
written paper and 50% marks in the aggregate, while no 
candidate shall be deemed to have qualified the 
examination unless he or she further secures at least 
30% marks in viva voce. The Appendix further 
stipulates that failure in, or absence from, viva voce 
shall render the candidate disqualified for appointment 
and his/her name shall not be included in the merit list. 
Equally significant is the declared object of the 
examination, which is stated to be the assessment of the 
practical ability and overall suitability of candidates 
rather than a mere evaluation of theoretical legal 
knowledge, while the viva voce is specifically designed 
to examine alertness, intelligence, general outlook, 
personality, bearing and other qualities essential for 
judicial office. A written examination and viva voce


# Page 11

11
serve different but complementary functions in the 
process of public recruitment. While the written 
examination provides an objective assessment of 
knowledge and intellectual competence, viva voce 
enables the assessment of personal qualities and 
attributes not amenable to written evaluation. Thus, the 
statutory framework treats the viva voce not as a 
ceremonial, ancillary, or optional exercise, but as an 
indispensable and substantive component of the 
competitive examination. It is, therefore, evident that a 
candidate who either does not participate in the viva 
voce or fails to secure the prescribed minimum 
qualifying threshold of 30% marks therein cannot be 
regarded as having qualified the examination. 
Consequently, no such candidate can claim any vested 
or enforceable right to recommendation, inclusion in 
the merit list, or appointment merely on the basis of 
having passed the written portion of the examination, 
as successful completion of both the written and viva 
voce stages is a mandatory precondition under the 
governing 
statutory 
framework. 
Therefore, 
the 
contention that aggregate written marks alone confer a 
right to inclusion in the merit list, or that viva voce 
cannot operate as a qualifying stage, stands squarely


# Page 12

12
contradicted by the express language of the Appendix, 
which makes successful completion of both the written 
and viva voce components a condition precedent for 
qualification and consideration for appointment. 
Significantly, neither the vires of the Rules nor the 
validity of the Appendix appended thereto has been 
challenged by the petitioners and the statutory scheme 
governing recruitment to the Judicial Service under the 
JSR 2001, having been framed in exercise of the 
powers conferred by Section 26 of the Khyber 
Pakhtunkhwa Civil Servants Act, 1973, enjoys full 
legal sanctity and binding force. 
8. 
As regards the contention that viva voce is 
inherently 
subjective 
and 
susceptible 
to 
bias, 
favouritism, inconsistency, or other extraneous 
considerations, there can be little dispute that oral 
interviews have historically attracted such criticism. 
However, 
such 
criticism, 
though 
not 
entirely 
unfounded, cannot justify the exclusion of viva voce 
from the selection process, particularly where the post 
in question requires qualities that cannot be adequately 
measured through written examinations alone. Judicial 
office is not merely an academic position requiring 
knowledge of law; rather, it is a public trust demanding


# Page 13

13
integrity, temperament, independence of mind, sound 
judgment, 
decisiveness, 
communication 
skills, 
composure, and the ability to perform judicial functions 
under pressure. These attributes are not readily 
amenable to objective evaluation through written 
papers and may legitimately be assessed through viva 
voce. Therefore, the mere presence of a subjective 
element in the interview process does not render it 
unconstitutional, arbitrary, or unlawful, especially 
when it forms an express and integral part of the 
governing statutory framework. What the law requires 
is not the elimination of discretion altogether, which is 
neither possible nor desirable, but the adoption of 
adequate safeguards to ensure fairness, transparency, 
and objectivity. Such safeguards may include the 
constitution of competent and experienced interview 
panels, independent assessment by each member, 
separate recording of individual marks, preservation of 
the assessment record, determination of the final result 
on the basis of collective evaluation, and disclosure of 
the interview marks secured by each candidate. These 
measures substantially reduce the possibility of 
arbitrariness and ensure that the assessment reflects the 
considered opinion of the panel as a whole. Therefore,


# Page 14

14
while viva voce may not be a wholly objective method 
of assessment, the solution lies in regulating and 
structuring the process rather than dispensing with it. 
Once viva voce constitutes a mandatory component of 
the statutory recruitment process, a candidate who fails 
to secure the prescribed qualifying threshold therein 
cannot claim any vested right to recommendation or 
appointment merely on the basis of performance in the 
written examination. 
9. 
Another contention advanced before the 
Court is that the interviewers, or the Interview Panel, 
did not properly evaluate the petitioners and failed to 
award marks proportionate to the marks obtained by 
them in the written examination. This contention is 
misconceived. It is a settled principle of jurisprudence, 
consistently recognized by the superior courts, that, 
ordinarily, the High Court, in the exercise of its 
constitutional jurisdiction, cannot assume the role of an 
appellate or supervisory authority over the examination 
and assessment process conducted by the Public 
Service Commission. The evaluation of candidates, 
including assessment in viva voce, falls within the 
exclusive domain of the Commission and its duly 
constituted Interview Panel, which are mandated under


# Page 15

15
the law to conduct interviews. Moreover, Regulation 
31(f) of the Khyber Pakhtunkhwa Public Service 
Commission Regulations, 2017 provides that interview 
proceedings are confidential and cannot be challenged. 
Unless mala fide, bias, patent illegality, or violation of 
the prescribed procedure is specifically established, the 
Court would not substitute its own opinion for that of 
the expert body entrusted with such function. At this 
juncture, it would be appropriate to examine the view 
expressed by the Honourable Supreme Court on the 
point in issue.  
1. Asif Mahmood Chughtai, Advocate v. Govt of 
Punjab (2000 SCMR 966): 
It was observed that high marks in the written 
examination do not confer a right to appointment where 
the candidate fails to secure the qualifying marks in 
interview.  
2. Dr. Mir Alam Jan V. Dr. Muhammad Shahzad 
(2008 SCMR 960): 
It was held that, in the exercise of its constitutional 
jurisdiction, the High Court is not expected to assume 
the role of a Selection Authority in service matters by 
substituting its own opinion for that of the competent 
authority.
