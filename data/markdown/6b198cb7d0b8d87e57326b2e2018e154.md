    JUDGMENT 
 
MUHAMMAD NAEEM ANWAR, J: - Muhammad Jahangir 
(respondent No.1) instituted a suit for declaration, asserting that 
the minor, Mst. Hooria, is his biological daughter. According to the 
plaint, defendant No.1, Muhammad Tufail (the petitioner herein), 
is his real brother who had remained issueless. It was alleged that, 
with the consent of the plaintiff and his wife, the petitioner was 
entrusted with the upbringing of the minor as an adopted child. 
However, taking undue advantage of such arrangement, the 
petitioner allegedly portrayed himself as the biological father of the 
minor and succeeded in recording his name as her father in the 
official records maintained by NADRA, educational institutions, 
and other public authorities. It was, therefore, prayed that the said 
entries be declared illegal, ineffective, and without lawful authority. 
The suit was contested by the petitioner, who categorically denied 
the plea of adoption and asserted that Mst. Hooria is his biological 
daughter. He maintained that all official documents, including the 
Child Registration Certificate (CRC/Form-B), Family Registration 
Certificate (FRC), educational record, and other public 
documents, correctly reflected him as the father of the minor. It 
was further pleaded that the suit had been instituted mala fide, 
solely to harass him, and was liable to dismissal.  
  
The remaining official defendants also resisted the claim by 
asserting that the Child Registration Certificate and allied public 
records had been prepared on the basis of the information 
furnished by Muhammad Tufail in the year 2016, long before the 
institution of the suit, and therefore the entries contained therein

2 
 
 
carried a presumption of correctness unless rebutted through 
cogent and convincing evidence.  
  
To substantiate his claim, the plaintiff examined 
Muhammad Sarwar, the real brother of both contesting parties, as 
PW-1, and Attiq-ur-Rehman as PW-2, besides appearing in the 
witness box himself as PW-3. Mst. Noreen also deposed in support 
of the plaintiff and stated that Mst. Hooria and one Robina were 
twin daughters and that, since Muhammad Tufail was childless, 
one of the twins, namely Mst. Hooria, was handed over to him for 
upbringing. In rebuttal, the petitioner produced Zulfiqar Khan as 
DW-1, examined himself in defence, while the minor Mst. Hooria 
also entered the witness box as DW-2. Zeeshan Akbar, Secretary, 
Union Council, was likewise examined in support of the defence. 
Upon appreciation of the oral and documentary evidence, the 
learned trial Court dismissed the suit. However, the learned 
Additional District Judge-I, Lahor, District Swabi, vide judgment 
and decree dated 23.01.2025, allowed the appeal and reversed the 
findings recorded by the trial Court. Hence, the present petition. 
2.  
 
I have heard the learned counsel for the parties at 
considerable length and perused the record. 
3. 
 
The documentary evidence produced on record 
overwhelmingly supports the case of the petitioner. The Child 
Registration Certificate (Ex. DW-1/1), Family Registration 
Certificate (Ex. DW-1/2), Birth Registration Certificate (Ex. DW-
1/3), educational record, and even the passport issued in favour of 
the minor on 05.12.2024 consistently describe Muhammad Tufail 
as her father and Nayab Tufail as her mother. Significantly, the 
Child Registration Certificate was issued on 19.08.2016, several 
years prior to the institution of the suit on 01.04.2022, and records 
the date of birth of the minor as 25.01.2014. Consequently, the 
official record demonstrates that the petitioner has continuously 
been recognized as the father of the minor since her early 
childhood, without any objection from the respondent for almost 
thirteen years. It is also an admitted position that from the date of 
her birth until today, the minor has continuously remained in the 
care, custody, and upbringing of the petitioner and his wife. She

3 
 
 
has been residing with them at Tarbela, pursuing her education 
there, and has throughout regarded them as her parents. This fact 
was candidly admitted even by PW, Mst. Noreen, during her cross-
examination. The plaintiff sought to rely upon the testimony of 
Muhammad Sarwar, who stated that Mst. Hooria and Robina were 
twins. However, no documentary evidence whatsoever was 
produced to substantiate such assertion. During the course of 
arguments, learned counsel for the respondent fairly conceded that 
Robina was born in a hospital, yet no hospital record, birth register, 
delivery record, medical certificate, or any contemporaneous 
document was produced to establish either the alleged twin birth 
or the biological parentage of the minor. Such omission assumes 
considerable significance because documentary evidence of birth 
was admittedly available but deliberately withheld. 
4. 
 
Equally devoid of legal efficacy is the reliance placed 
upon a Fatwa produced by the respondent during the proceedings. 
The determination of biological parentage is essentially a question 
of fact requiring adjudication on legally admissible evidence. Such 
an issue cannot be decided on the basis of a religious opinion or 
Fatwa, however respectable its source may be, as it neither 
constitutes substantive evidence nor displaces duly maintained 
public records. 
5. 
 
Although modern scientific techniques, including 
DNA profiling, may in an appropriate case provide relevant 
evidence regarding biological parentage, such evidence is not 
conclusive in every circumstance. In the present case, the 
contesting parties are real brothers and, therefore, share a 
substantial degree of genetic similarity. Consequently, even a DNA 
examination could not, by itself, conclusively resolve the 
controversy unless supported by other independent and 
convincing evidence. Be that as it may, no application seeking 
DNA analysis was ever filed before the learned trial Court, nor 
does any such evidence form part of the record. The appellate 
Court, therefore, had no lawful basis to draw any inference on that 
account.

6. 
 
Another significant aspect of the matter relates to the 
age, welfare, and psychological well-being of the minor herself. At 
present, Mst. Hooria is approximately thirteen years of age. She 
has spent her entire conscious life in the custody, care, affection, 
and guardianship of the petitioner and his wife. She has been 
educated under their care, her official identity has consistently 
reflected them as her parents, and she herself appeared before the 
Court affirming that relationship. Any judicial determination 
unsettling that settled parentage at this advanced stage of her 
childhood would inevitably expose the minor to severe emotional 
distress, psychological trauma, social embarrassment, and identity 
crisis. The Court, while adjudicating disputes affecting minors, 
cannot remain oblivious to the paramount consideration of their 
welfare, emotional stability, and best interests. 
7. 
 
The significant aspect the reply of Muhammad 
Jahangir when questioned regarding the extraordinary delay in 
instituting the suit, he admitted that relations between the two 
brothers had initially remained cordial but had subsequently 
become strained. This admission is of considerable significance. It 
unmistakably indicates that the dispute regarding the parentage of 
the minor surfaced only after the deterioration of relations between 
the brothers. Such strained personal relations can never furnish a 
legitimate foundation for unsettling the identity and status of a child 
who has, for over a decade, lived under a settled family 
environment. 
8. 
 
The respondent's decision to question the parentage 
of the minor after an unconscionable delay of nearly thirteen years 
is wholly inconsistent with ordinary human conduct. Had he 
genuinely believed himself to be the biological father, nothing 
prevented him from asserting such a claim immediately after the 
birth of the child or at least when the Child Registration Certificate, 
Family Registration Certificate, school records, and other official 
documents were prepared in the petitioner's name years earlier. 
His prolonged silence, coupled with the institution of the present 
proceedings only after the relationship between the brothers 
became hostile, substantially undermines the credibility of his

5 
 
 
claim and strongly suggests that the litigation is a manifestation of 
personal animosity rather than a bona fide effort to vindicate a legal 
right. 
9. 
 
In addition, under Article 46(5) of the Qanun-e-
Shahadat Order, 1984, statements relating to the existence of a 
relationship by blood, marriage, or adoption are relevant provided 
they are made by a person having special means of knowledge 
before any dispute concerning such relationship has arisen. 
Likewise, Article 64, Illustration (b), recognizes that where a person 
has consistently been treated by members of the family as the 
legitimate child of another, such conduct constitutes a relevant 
circumstance for determining questions of parentage. These 
provisions underscore the legislative policy that, in disputes 
concerning status and parentage, contemporaneous declarations, 
family conduct, and long-standing recognition assume considerable 
evidentiary significance. The documentary evidence assumes even 
greater importance where it emanates from official records 
maintained by public authorities in the ordinary course of their 
statutory functions. The evidentiary value of a birth certificate has 
long been recognized by judicial precedent for which reference 
may be made to the cases of Mst. Zainab Jehan v. Additional 
District Judge (2012 YLR 1480), Roshan Din v. Mst. Ghulam 
Fatima (2002 YLR 2706). In the present case, the petitioner’s 
strongest documentary evidence consists of the production of the 
original Birth certificate, form B of NADRA, School record 
brought of record which carry a statutory presumption of 
correctness and cannot be lightly discarded except on the basis of 
cogent, convincing, and unimpeachable evidence establishing 
fraud, fabrication, or material inaccuracy. The same principle 
governs entries made in identity documents maintained by 
statutory authorities. In Razia Khatoon v. Additional District 
Judge (1991 SCMR 840), the Hon'ble Supreme Court held that, in 
the absence of convincing rebuttal, the entries contained in the 
National Identity Card are to prevail and observed that: "In such 
background the entries of the National Identity Card shall hold the 
field unless they are rebutted by an equally good or better

6 
 
 
evidence." Which principle was reaffirmed in Abdul Ghani Khan 
v. Muhammad Ibrahim (2011 SCMR 837), wherein the august 
Supreme Court further emphasized that documentary evidence 
cannot be displaced by bare oral assertions, observing that where 
official documentary evidence remains unrebutted, mere verbal 
claims are legally insufficient to negate its probative value. Reliance 
was placed upon Akhtar Hussain Zaidi v. Muhammad 
Yaqinuddin (1988 SCMR 753), wherein it was authoritatively held 
that oral assertions, unsupported by equally reliable documentary 
evidence, cannot prevail over authentic public records. The 
cumulative effect of the above statutory provisions and judicial 
pronouncements is that, in disputes relating to parentage, official 
records such as birth registers, birth certificates, Child Registration 
Certificates, 
National 
Identity 
Cards, 
and 
other 
contemporaneously prepared public documents constitute the 
most reliable category of evidence. Once such documentary 
evidence consistently records the parentage of a child, the burden 
shifts heavily upon the party challenging those entries to rebut them 
by evidence of equal or superior probative value. Mere oral 
assertions, belated claims, or family disputes surfacing long after 
the creation of official records do not possess sufficient evidentiary 
weight to displace the statutory presumption of correctness 
attached to public documents maintained in the ordinary course of 
official business. Although the plaintiff/respondent has challenged 
the authenticity and correctness of the entire documentary record, 
he has failed to produce any cogent, convincing, credible, or 
unimpeachable evidence capable of rebutting the presumption 
attached to those public documents. In the absence of evidence of 
equal or superior probative value, the official records continue to 
hold the field, and the respondent's bare oral assertions are wholly 
insufficient in law to invalidate or override the consistent 
documentary evidence on record. 
10. 
 
Courts of law are guardians not only of legal rights 
but also of the dignity, security, and welfare of minors. Children 
must never be converted into instruments for settling personal 
vendettas or family rivalries. To permit the respondent, after

7 
 
 
allowing the child to remain with the petitioner throughout her 
formative years, to reopen the issue of her parentage merely 
because of subsequent hostility between two brothers would be to 
sacrifice the welfare of the child at the altar of personal acrimony. 
Such an approach would expose the minor to lasting psychological 
scars, undermine her emotional security, disrupt her social identity, 
and irreparably impair her future development. Equity, good 
conscience, and the doctrine of acquiescence equally militate 
against entertaining such a destabilizing claim after the lapse of 
more than a decade. 
11. 
 
The learned appellate Court, while reversing the 
well-reasoned findings of the trial Court, primarily relied upon the 
oral testimony of the plaintiff and his witnesses. However, such oral 
assertions fall far short of the standard required to dislodge 
consistent public documents carrying a statutory presumption of 
correctness. No convincing evidence regarding pregnancy, 
delivery, contemporaneous medical treatment, birth registration, 
or any other independent circumstance establishing biological 
parentage was produced. Mere oral assertions, unsupported by 
reliable documentary evidence, cannot prevail over official records 
consistently maintained since the infancy of the child. Likewise, 
undue emphasis was placed by the appellate Court on the non-
appearance of the petitioner's wife. Such reasoning is legally 
unsustainable. The burden of proving the alleged biological 
parentage squarely rested upon the plaintiff, who approached the 
Court seeking a declaration contrary to long-standing official 
records. Weakness, if any, in the defence cannot relieve the 
plaintiff of his primary obligation to establish his own case through 
cogent, convincing, and unimpeachable evidence. That burden has 
remained wholly undischarged. 
12. 
 
Viewed from every conceivable angle, the entire 
documentary record, the admitted long custody of the minor, the 
unexplained delay of nearly thirteen years, the absence of reliable 
evidence regarding birth, the respondent's own admission 
regarding strained relations between the brothers, and above all, 
the paramount welfare and emotional security of the minor,


# Page 8

8 
 
 
overwhelmingly support the findings recorded by the learned trial 
Court. The learned appellate Court, therefore, fell into patent 
error in reversing those findings on the basis of insufficient and 
unreliable evidence, resulting in a miscarriage of justice. 
13.  
 
For the foregoing reasons, the instant petition merits 
acceptance and is accordingly allowed. The judgment and decree 
dated 23.01.2025 passed by the learned Additional District Judge-
I, Lahor, District Swabi, are hereby set aside, while the judgment 
and decree of the learned trial Court dismissing the suit are 
restored. No order as to costs.
