10. Before placing reliance upon the statement of 
deceased 
Faizan 
Zeb 
embodied 
in 
the 
Murasila/FIR, it would be appropriate to discuss 
the legal principles governing evidentiary value of 
dying declaration. A dying declaration is an 
exception to the general rule excluding hearsay 
evidence and has been made admissible under


# Page 8

8
Article 46 of the Qanun-e-Shahadat Order, 1984 
on the principle that a person standing at the brink 
of death is not likely to falsely implicate an 
innocent person. The law consistently recognizes 
that when a statement is made by a deceased 
person relating to cause of his death or 
circumstances resulting in his death, such 
statement 
becomes 
relevant 
and 
admissible 
provided the same inspires confidence and is 
proved in accordance with law. 
11. The august Supreme Court of Pakistan in the 
case of “Abdul Khaliq versus The State” reported 
as 2021 SCMR 325 held that where a deceased 
was conscious and medically fit to make statement 
and the same was recorded immediately after 
occurrence, 
such 
dying 
declaration 
carries 
substantial evidentiary value and can safely be 
relied upon if surrounding circumstances establish 
its truthfulness. Likewise, in “Majeed versus The 
State” reported as 2010 SCMR 55 and “Nazeer 
Khan versus The State and another” reported as 
2019 SCMR 1308, the august Supreme Court 
recognized the evidentiary sanctity of dying


# Page 9

9
declaration where circumstances establish that the 
same was made voluntarily and truthfully. 
12. It is equally settled that a dying declaration 
need not necessarily be made under immediate 
expectation of death. In “Wazir Gul versus The 
State” reported as 1976 SCMR 471 and “Zarif 
Khan versus The State” reported as PLD 1977 
Supreme Court 612, the august Supreme Court 
held that expectation of immediate death is not a 
sine qua non for admissibility of dying declaration. 
Similarly, FIR lodged by injured person himself, 
who subsequently succumbs to injuries sustained 
during occurrence, can legally be treated as dying 
declaration if proved in accordance with law. 
Reference in this regard may be made to 1990 
SCMR 588, PLD 2003 Supreme Court 635, 
PLD 2006 Supreme Court 186 and 2011 PCr.LJ 
1455. 
13. The principles for determining genuineness 
and 
reliability 
of 
dying 
declaration 
have 
consistently been elaborated by superior Courts. In 
the celebrated judgment rendered by the Hon’ble 
Lahore High Court in “Taj Muhammad and


# Page 10

10
others versus The State” reported as PLD 1960 
Lahore 723, it was held that if a dying declaration 
is found to be genuine and true, it can by itself 
form sufficient basis for conviction. It was further 
held that some of the principal tests for judging 
veracity 
of 
dying 
declaration 
are 
whether 
intrinsically it rings true, whether there existed any 
chance of mistaken identity, whether the maker of 
declaration was physically and mentally capable of 
making statement, whether the statement was free 
from prompting or tutoring from outside influence 
and whether the same remained consistent with 
other evidence and circumstances of the case. 
14. Likewise, this Court in “Zabta Khan versus 
The State” reported as PLD 1963 (W.P.) 
Peshawar 66 adopted and reiterated the above 
principles. Similarly, in “Abdur Rahim versus The 
State” reported as 1997 PCr.LJ 1274, the criteria 
for 
evaluating 
evidentiary 
worth 
of 
dying 
declaration was further elaborated in the following 
terms:- 
(i) Whether there was no chance of mistaken 
identity;


# Page 11

11
(ii) Whether deceased was capable of making 
statement; 
(iii) After how much time of sustaining injury the 
statement was made; 
(iv) Whether the statement rings true; 
(v) Whether it was free from prompting or outside 
influence; and 
(vi) Whether 
deceased 
was 
a 
person 
of 
questionable character. 
 
15. The august Supreme Court of Pakistan in 
“Muhammad Afzal versus Faqir and others” 
reported as PLD 1984 Supreme Court 375 further 
observed that merely because injured person had 
sustained serious injuries, it cannot automatically 
be presumed that he was incapable of making 
statement, particularly where medical evidence 
establishes that injured remained conscious and fit 
to communicate. The august Supreme Court 
emphasized that medical opinion and surrounding 
circumstances are of decisive importance while 
assessing truthfulness of dying declaration. 
16. Similarly, in “Abdul Razik versus The State” 
reported as PLD 1965 Supreme Court 151, it was 
held that though dying declaration is admissible 
under law, however, its acceptance or rejection


# Page 12

12
ultimately depends upon judicial evaluation based 
upon 
ordinary 
human 
conduct, 
surrounding 
circumstances and intrinsic worth of statement 
itself.

31. However, despite the fact that prosecution 
succeeded in proving occurrence and participation 
of accused persons, the crucial question requiring 
determination is whether the case falls within 
ambit of premeditated murder attracting extreme 
penalty or whether mitigating circumstances are 
available warranting lesser sentence. Admittedly, 
prosecution itself alleged that occurrence had taken 
place all of a sudden on a trivial issue. The FIR 
itself reveals that altercation and abuses started 
between parties at spot and thereafter unfortunate 
occurrence took place. No previous motive or 
enmity has been attributed by prosecution. Entire 
occurrence appears to have taken place at spur of 
moment without preplanning or premeditation. 
32. It is also noteworthy that appellant Syed

33. We are therefore of the considered view that 
present case is not one of extreme penalty. 
Occurrence admittedly took place suddenly during
