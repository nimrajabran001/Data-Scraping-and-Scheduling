The first and perhaps most fundamental 
objection raised on behalf of the respondents is that the suit 
itself was barred by Section 172 of the West Pakistan Land 
Revenue Act, 1967 and, therefore, the Civil Court lacked 
jurisdiction to examine the validity of the notification. 
10. 
The argument, though attractive at first glance, 
does not withstand close examination. Sections 53 and 172 
of the Act of 1967 being relevant are given below:

11. Section 172 of the Act bars the jurisdiction of Civil 
Courts only in respect of matters which the revenue 
authorities are empowered by the Act to determine. The 
provision was never intended to place beyond judicial 
scrutiny every action purportedly taken under the Act. To 
interpret it otherwise would render even patently illegal, 
mala fide or jurisdictionally defective actions immune 
from challenge, a consequence which the law has never 
countenanced. 
12. 
It is now well settled that exclusion of the 
jurisdiction of Civil Courts is not to be readily inferred.


# Page 6

6
The jurisdiction of ordinary Courts remains intact unless 
expressly or by necessary implication taken away. Even 
where a statutory bar exists, Civil Courts continue to 
exercise jurisdiction where the impugned action is alleged 
to be without jurisdiction, mala fide, coram non judice, or 
in excess of powers conferred by statute. 
13. 
In Muhammad Jamil Asghar v. Improvement 
Trust, Rawalpindi (PLD 1965 SC 698), the Honourable 
Supreme Court held that exclusion of the jurisdiction of 
Civil Courts is not to be lightly presumed and that Civil 
Courts remain competent to examine whether a statutory 
authority has acted within the limits of the law creating it. 
14. 
Likewise, in Hamid Hussain v. Government of 
West Pakistan (1974 SCMR 356), it was held that where 
an authority acts beyond the powers conferred upon it by 
law, the protection otherwise available under a statutory 
bar ceases to operate. 
15. The principle was reaffirmed in Muhammad Nazir 
Khan v. Ahmad and others (2008 SCMR 521), wherein the 
Honourable Supreme Court held that where allegations of 
mala fide, lack of jurisdiction or violation of vested rights 
are raised, the Civil Court is competent to examine the 
legality of the impugned action notwithstanding a statutory 
bar. It was observed: 
“…Thirdly, the learned Single Judge after properly 
adverting to the principles of law laid down by this 
Court in the case of Alam Sher through Legal heirs 
v. Muhammad Sharif and 2 others 1998 SCMR 468

7
and taking into consideration the facts of the instant 
case held that the action of respondents Nos.2 and 3 
is 
without 
jurisdiction 
and 
void, 
therefore, 
respondent No.1 was within his 'rights to maintain 
the suit before the Civil Courts, the bar of section 36 
of the Colonization of Government Lands (Punjab) 
Act, 1912 does not apply and the Civil Court could 
competently proceed to adjudicate and entertain the 
suit; Lastly, this Court in a case reported as Hamid, 
Hussain v. Government of West Pakistan and others 
1974 SCMR 356 while relying upon an earlier 
judgment of this Court in the case of Muhammad 
Jamil Asghar v. Improvement Trust PLD 1965 SC 
698 has held as under: 
  
"(b) Jurisdiction--- 
----Jurisdiction of Civil Courts even if barred and 
conferred upon special Tribunals, Civil Courts being 
Courts of ultimate jurisdiction have jurisdiction to 
examine acts of such forums to see if such are in 
accordance with law, or illegal, or even mala fide."” 
 
 
Thus, it is now firmly settled that where mala fide, excess 
of powers, transgression of jurisdiction, colourable 
exercise of authority or ultra vires action is alleged, the 
Civil Court remains the Court of ultimate jurisdiction 
competent to examine the validity of the impugned action. 
16. 
More recently, the same principle was reiterated 
in Sher Ali Kakar v. Director General Sports and Youth 
Affairs, 
Government 
of

protection extends only to lawful exercise of power and 
cannot be invoked to shield actions that are ultra vires the 
statute itself. 
17. 
The present case falls squarely within the 
recognized exceptions. The petitioners do not merely 
challenge individual entries in the revenue record. Their


# Page 8

8
attack is directed against the very foundation of the 
exercise, namely the notification issued under Section 40. 
They contend that the notification was issued without 
lawful basis, without jurisdiction, without publication in 
the Official Gazette, without notice to affected persons, 
and in disregard of longstanding rights already recognised 
by judicial determinations. Such allegations, if established, 
unquestionably attract the jurisdiction of the Civil Court. 
18. 
There is yet another reason why the objection to 
jurisdiction cannot succeed. Section 53 of the West 
Pakistan Land Revenue Act, 1967 expressly provides that 
any person considering himself aggrieved by an entry in a 
record of rights or annual record may institute a suit for 
declaration under Chapter VI of the Specific Relief Act, 
1877. This statutory remedy is further preserved by the 
opening words of Section 172, namely, “Except as 
otherwise provided by this Act”. Thus, where a person is 
aggrieved by entries incorporated in the record pursuant to 
an impugned exercise under the Act, the remedy expressly 
recognised by Section 53 remains available and stands 
excluded from the bar contemplated by Section 172. 
19. 
The objection based upon Section 172, therefore, 
cannot be accepted. 
20. 
This brings us to the nature and scope of Section 
40 itself. The same is given below for convenience:

40. (1) When it appears to the Board of Revenue that 
a record-of-rights for an estate does not exist, or that 
the existing record-of-rights for an estate requires

(2) A notification under sub-section (1) may direct

(3) A record-of-rights made or specially revised for

(Underlining supplied) 
 
21. 
Section 40 empowers the Board of Revenue to 
direct special revision of a record of rights where 
circumstances justify such a course. The provision 
undoubtedly confers an important supervisory power. Yet 
the power is neither unbridled nor unfettered. It is an 
extraordinary jurisdiction intended to ensure accuracy and 
reliability of revenue records. It was never designed as a 
mechanism for reopening settled rights, nullifying judicial 
findings, or altering proprietary interests through executive 
fiat. 
22. 
The distinction is important. A record of rights 
records existing rights; it does not create them. Likewise, 
revision of a record of rights is intended to ensure 
correctness of entries; it is not a substitute for adjudication 
of disputed title.


# Page 10

10
23. 
In Muhammad Yaqoob and others v. Abdul 
Ghani and others (1983 SCMR 1222), the Honourable 
Supreme Court held: 
“In so far as the exercise of power under section 
40 is concerned, the revision of the record of 
rights is relatable to the justification found in 
each case and that depends upon the merits 
thereof.” 
 
The authority of the Board to act under Section 40 is, 
therefore, not immune from judicial scrutiny. The Court 
remains entitled to examine whether the circumstances of 
the particular case furnished lawful justification for 
invocation of that power. The existence of statutory power 
does not by itself conclude the inquiry. The Court remains 
entitled to examine whether the circumstances justified 
invocation of that power. 
24. 
Viewed in that light, the central question is not 
whether the Board of Revenue possessed power under 
Section 40. It undoubtedly did. The real question is 
whether the circumstances of the present case justified 
invocation of that extraordinary jurisdiction.

41. 
Longstanding entries incorporated in successive 
Jamabandis carry a statutory presumption of correctness. 
Such entries are not lightly disturbed. The law attaches 
significance to continuity in the record because rights,

16
transactions and expectations are often arranged on the

42. 
Since much of the respondents' case appears to 
have rested on inferences drawn from Lagan/Kaifiyat 
entries rather than ownership and possession columns, it is 
important to note that it is equally well settled that the 
statutory presumption attaches to entries in the columns of 
ownership and possession and not to entries in the columns 
of Lagan or Kaifiyat. In Mira Khan v. Abdul Ghawas (PLD 
1994 Peshawar 209), this Court held that where entries in 
the column of rent conflict with entries in the column of 
possession, the latter prevail. It was observed: 
“The suit of Mira Khan based on title was proved 
from the revenue record on file wherein Abdul 
Ghawas, etc. the respondents/defendants were 
entered as tenants‑at­ will but in the column of 
Lagan the entry appeared to be that of "Bila Lagan 
Bawaja‑e‑Baih". There is not an iota of evidence 
regarding such sale and even otherwise when the 
entries in the column of rent differ from the entries 
in the column of possession, those in the column of 
possession would prevail.” 
 
43. 
More recently, in Hasham Khan through LRs v. 
Waheed Ahmed (2024 SCMR 353), the Honourable 
Supreme Court reaffirmed that presumption of correctness 
attaches only to the columns of ownership and possession 
in the record of rights and not to entries in the column of 
Lagan or Kaifiyat, which must be independently proved. It 
was held: 
“…Lahore High Court in the judgment reported as 
"Pervez Alam Khan v. Muhammad Mukhtar Khan" 
(2001 CLC 1489) while relying on the judgment of 
this court reported as "Shad Muhammad v. Khan

presumption of correctness is attached only to the

made in Khana Kafiyat of Register of Record of 
Rights/Jamabadi. Whenever, a party relies on this 
Column, they will have to prove the incorporated 
statement/entry through independent evidence.”

There is yet another aspect of the case which 
strikes at the very root of the impugned exercise. It is an 
admitted position on the record that the notification dated 
28.08.1998 was never published in the Official Gazette. 
57. 
The petitioners contend that publication in the 
Official Gazette was a mandatory prerequisite to the legal 
efficacy and enforceability of the notification and that, in 
the absence of such publication, the notification never 
acquired lawful force. The respondents, on the other hand, 
argue that publication was merely procedural and that non-
publication does not necessarily invalidate the exercise. 
58. 
The issue is of considerable importance and 
warrants careful examination in light of the statutory 
framework as well as the jurisprudence developed by the 
superior Courts.

22
59. 
Section 2(41) of the West Pakistan General 
Clauses Act, 1956 defines a “notification” in the following 
terms: 
“Notification” shall mean a notification published 
under proper authority in the official Gazette. 
 
 
60. 
The statutory definition itself is significant. It 
does not merely describe a notification as a decision, order 
or declaration issued by the Government. Rather, it 
expressly links the very concept of a notification with 
publication in the Official Gazette. Publication is thus 
embedded within the statutory definition itself. 
61. 
The question nevertheless remains whether non-
publication invariably renders a notification void or 
whether the consequence depends upon the nature of the 
statute and the rights affected. 
62. 
The earlier and stricter view is reflected in 
Muhammad Suleman and others v. Abdul Ghani and 
others (PLD 1978 SC 190), wherein the Honourable 
Supreme Court held that a notification derives its legal 
character from publication in the Official Gazette and that 
mere issuance is not sufficient. This principle was 
subsequently reaffirmed in Government of Sindh v. Khan 
Ginners (Pvt.) Ltd. (PLD 2011 SC 347), wherein the 
Supreme Court observed: 
“The case of Muhammad Suleman and others v. 
Abdul Ghani PLD 1978 SC 190 throws sufficient 
light on the legal position that issuance of a 
Notification is not of any significance or legal


# Page 23

23
importance till it is published in an official Gazette. 
According to section 2(41) of the General Clauses 
Act, 1956 a ‘Notification’ means a Notification 
published under proper authority in an official 
Gazette. In this view of the matter before its 
publication in the official Gazette the Notification 
could not even be lawfully termed as a Notification.” 
 
63. 
The jurisprudence, however, did not remain 
static. In Saghir Ahmed v. Province of Punjab (PLD 2004 
SC 261), the Supreme Court observed that no universal 
rule could be laid down regarding the consequences of 
non-publication and that the answer would depend upon 
the language, object and purpose of the particular statute. 
The Court explained that provisions regarding publication 
may, in some situations, be directory rather than 
mandatory. This approach was subsequently followed in 
Bahadur Khan v. Federation of Pakistan (2017 SCMR 
2066), where it was held that non-publication does not 
invariably deprive a statutory instrument of its legal status. 
64. 
The 
apparent 
divergence 
was 
ultimately 
reconciled by the larger Bench of the Honourable Supreme 
Court in Justice Qazi Faez Isa and others v. President of 
Pakistan and others (PLD 2021 SC 1). After reviewing the 
earlier authorities, the Court held that no hard and fast rule 
of universal application could be formulated. However, 
two important exceptions were recognised. First, where the 
governing statute expressly requires publication in the 
Official Gazette; and second, where the rights and 
liabilities of persons are affected. In such situations,


# Page 24

24
publication assumes mandatory significance. The Court 
expressly reiterated the observations made in Saghir 
Ahmed that provisions relating to publication may be 
treated as mandatory “where rights or liabilities of other 
persons are involved.” 
65. 
The modern jurisprudence has consistently 
moved in that direction. In Controller General of Accounts 
v. Fazil Ahmad (2021 SCMR 800), the Honourable 
Supreme Court declined to give legal effect to a 
notification which had neither been issued by the 
competent authority nor published in the Official Gazette. 
66. 
Likewise, in Province of Punjab v. Syed Zia-ul-
Hassan Zaidi and others (2022 SCMR 830), the 
Honourable Supreme Court held that publication of a 
notification was mandatory where acquisition proceedings 
were likely to affect the rights and interests of landowners. 
The Court emphasised that publication serves the vital 
purpose of informing affected persons and enabling them 
to avail the safeguards provided by law. 
67. 
More recently, in Commissioner Inland Revenue 
and others v. Shiraz Restaurant and others (2023 SCMR 
2084), the Honourable Supreme Court once again 
underscored 
the 
importance 
of 
transparency 
in 
governmental action and observed that persons affected by 
the exercise of public power must know who is exercising


# Page 25

25
authority and under what lawful source such authority is 
claimed. 
68. 
The legal position emerging from the above 
authorities may be summarised thus: publication in the 
Official Gazette is not invariably mandatory for every 
governmental act. However, where the governing statute 
contemplates publication, or where the notification is 
intended to affect, alter, diminish or determine the rights 
and liabilities of citizens, publication ceases to be a matter 
of form and becomes a substantive requirement. In such 
cases, publication serves the fundamental purposes of 
legality, transparency, notice and due process. 
69. 
The present case falls squarely within that 
category. 
70. 
The impugned notification was purportedly 
issued under Section 40 of the West Pakistan Land 
Revenue Act, 1967 for special revision of the record of 
rights relating to Revenue Estate Kaghan. The exercise was 
not administrative in the narrow sense. Its very purpose 
was to alter entries affecting proprietary, possessory and 
tenancy rights recorded in the revenue record. Indeed, the 
entire controversy before this Court arises precisely 
because valuable civil rights were affected by the 
consequential changes carried out pursuant to the 
notification.


# Page 26

26
71. 
The case, therefore, satisfies both conditions 
recognised in PLD 2021 SC 1. First, the statutory 
definition contained in Section 2(41) of the West Pakistan 
General Clauses Act expressly contemplates publication in 
the Official Gazette. Secondly, the rights and liabilities of 
citizens were directly and substantially affected. 
72. 
In these circumstances, publication was not a 
procedural formality. It was an essential prerequisite to the 
legal efficacy and enforceability of the notification. 
73. 
A document which has not been published in the 
Official Gazette, despite being intended to alter vested 
proprietary and possessory rights, cannot readily be 
accorded the status of a legally operative notification 
capable of sustaining the drastic consequences that 
followed. 
74. 
The defect assumes even greater significance 
when viewed alongside the other infirmities already 
noticed. The local revenue authorities did not recommend 
special revision. The Deputy Commissioner did not 
recommend special revision. The Board of Revenue itself 
initially doubted applicability of Section 40. No reasons 
were recorded in the notification. The scope of the exercise 
was subsequently enlarged through a corrigendum. 
Longstanding entries reflected in successive Jamabandis 
were sought to be disturbed notwithstanding previous 
litigation concerning the same subject matter. The affected

75. 
Each of these circumstances independently 
raises a serious legal infirmity. Viewed collectively, they 
leave little room for doubt that the impugned notification 
and the consequential exercise undertaken pursuant thereto 
travelled beyond the lawful scope of Section 40 and cannot

76. 
The cumulative circumstances noticed above

not 
contemplated 
by 
the 
statute 
itself. 
Longstanding rights reflected in the record for decades and 
previously upheld through judicial proceedings were

Such exercise cannot be reconciled with the object, scope 
and limits of Section 40 and constitutes a colourable 
exercise of statutory authority.

The

Civil Court jurisdiction despite statutory bar – Section 172 of the Act bars the 
jurisdiction of Civil Courts only in respect of matters which the revenue 
authorities are empowered by the Act to determine. The provision was never 
intended to place beyond judicial scrutiny every action purportedly taken under 
the Act. Even where a statutory bar exists, Civil Courts continue to exercise 
jurisdiction where the impugned action is alleged to be without jurisdiction, mala 
fide, coram non judice, or in excess of powers conferred by statute. Where mala 
fide, excess of powers, transgression of jurisdiction, colourable exercise of 
authority or ultra vires action is alleged, the Civil Court remains the Court of 
ultimate jurisdiction competent to examine the validity of the impugned action. 
Sections 53 and 172, read together, preserve the right of a person aggrieved by 
entries in the record of rights to institute a suit for declaration. (Paras 11, 12, 15 
& 18) 
 
Section 40 – Scope and limits of special revision – Section 40 confers an 
extraordinary supervisory jurisdiction intended to ensure accuracy and reliability 
of revenue records. It was never designed as a mechanism for reopening settled 
rights, nullifying judicial findings, or altering proprietary interests through 
executive fiat. A record of rights records existing rights; it does not create them. 
Revision of a record of rights is intended to ensure correctness of entries; it is not 
a substitute for adjudication of disputed title. The validity of an exercise under 
Section 40 depends upon the justification found in each case and remains subject 
to judicial scrutiny. (Paras 21, 22 & 23) 
 
Notification under Section 40 – No recommendation by revenue authorities 
– No reasons recorded – Jurisdictional defect – The local revenue authorities 
did not recommend special revision. The Deputy Commissioner did not 
recommend special revision. The Board of Revenue itself initially doubted 
applicability of Section 40. Yet, notwithstanding these reservations, the 
impugned notification came to be issued. The notification itself furnished little 
assistance in explaining why the extraordinary jurisdiction under Section 40 was 
invoked. No reason whatsoever was disclosed. The notification neither identified 
any defect in the existing record nor referred to any fraud, mistake, irregularity, 
inquiry report, complaint, misrepresentation or other circumstance necessitating 
invocation of such extraordinary power. The notification recorded the conclusion 
but not the reasoning. (Paras 27, 28, 32 & 34) 
 
Corrigendum – Expansion from village to estate – Effect – A notification 
under Section 40 contemplates special revision of the record of rights of a 
revenue estate and not of particular persons, selected khasra numbers or isolated 
parcels of land. The effect of the corrigendum was not merely textual but 
substantive, as it sought to bring the notification within the statutory framework 
of Section 40. Even after issuance of the corrigendum, the exercise did not 
culminate in a general revision of the record of rights of the entire estate. Instead, 
the consequential changes remained confined to specific entries affecting the 
rights of the petitioners. The corrigendum reinforced the impression that the 
process ultimately adopted was neither transparent nor consistent with the object, 
scope and requirements of Section 40 of the Act. (Paras 37 & 38) 
 
Revenue record – Longstanding entries – Presumption of correctness – 
Longstanding entries incorporated in successive Jamabandis carry a statutory 
presumption of correctness. Such entries are not lightly disturbed. Revenue 
entries may not be conclusive proof of title, yet longstanding entries repeatedly 
carried forward in successive settlements and Jamabandis cannot be brushed 
aside without compelling justification. It is equally well settled that the statutory

30
presumption attaches to entries in the columns of ownership and possession and 
not to entries in the columns of Lagan or Kaifiyat. (Paras 41, 42, 43, 45 & 46) 
 
Administrative action vis-à-vis prior judicial determinations – Rights and 
entries that have survived judicial scrutiny over several tiers of adjudication 
acquire a degree of finality which cannot lightly be disregarded by executive 
action. While the executive may exercise powers conferred by statute, it cannot, 
under the guise of administrative action, nullify or circumvent findings rendered 
by competent Courts. What could not be achieved through litigation cannot 
subsequently be achieved through administrative action. (Paras 49, 50, 51 & 52) 
 
Notification – Publication in Official Gazette – Effect of non-publication – 
Evolution of jurisprudence – Section 2(41) of the West Pakistan General 
Clauses Act, 1956 expressly links the very concept of a notification with 
publication in the Official Gazette. The legal position emerging from the 
authorities is that publication in the Official Gazette is not invariably mandatory 
for every governmental act. However, where the governing statute contemplates 
publication, or where the notification is intended to affect, alter, diminish or 
determine the rights and liabilities of citizens, publication ceases to be a matter of 
form and becomes a substantive requirement. In such cases, publication serves 
the fundamental purposes of legality, transparency, notice and due process. 
(Paras 59, 60, 64 & 68) 
 
Notification affecting proprietary and possessory rights – Publication 
mandatory – The impugned notification was intended to alter entries affecting 
proprietary, possessory and tenancy rights recorded in the revenue record. The 
case satisfied both conditions recognised by the Supreme Court: first, the 
statutory definition expressly contemplated publication in the Official Gazette; 
secondly, the rights and liabilities of citizens were directly and substantially 
affected. In these circumstances, publication was not a procedural formality. It 
was an essential prerequisite to the legal efficacy and enforceability of the 
notification. A document which has not been published in the Official Gazette, 
despite being intended to alter vested proprietary and possessory rights, cannot 
readily be accorded the status of a legally operative notification capable of 
sustaining the drastic consequences that followed. (Paras 70, 71, 72 & 73)

statutory authority – The local revenue authorities did not recommend special
