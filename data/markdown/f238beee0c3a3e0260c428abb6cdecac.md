JUDGMENT 
SAHIBZADA ASADULLAH, J.-        Through the instant 
Criminal Revision, the petitioner has called in question the 
order dated 10.02.2026 passed by the learned trial Court, 
whereby the objection raised by the learned counsel for the 
complainant was accepted and permission was granted to 
cross-examine the witness on the ground that he had resiled 
from 
and 
deviated 
from 
his 
earlier 
statement. 
Consequently, the complainant was allowed to treat the 
witness as hostile and to confront him through cross-
examination. 
2.   
The present case raises questions that travel 
beyond the confines of a procedural provision and touch 
the very philosophy of the law of evidence. Courts have, 
through centuries, grappled with a recurring difficulty: 
what is to be done when a witness, having entered the 
witness-box under the expectation that he would assist the


# Page 2

2
Court in the ascertainment of truth, abandons his earlier 
position, suppresses material facts, or assumes an attitude 
averse to the party who summoned him? It is from this 
practical challenge that the doctrine of a hostile witness 
emerged and gradually evolved into a recognized 
instrument of judicial administration. 
3.   
The controversy before this Court is not merely 
whether a witness may be declared hostile, but also who 
may seek such a declaration, what circumstances justify the 
exercise of such power, and whether the jurisdiction of the 
Court is confined to the stage of examination-in-chief or 
extends to any point during the witness's testimony, 
including cross-examination where the witness first 
manifests hostility or materially departs from his previous 
version. Resolution of these questions necessarily requires 
an examination of the historical origins of the doctrine, the 
rationale underlying its incorporation into modern 
evidentiary law, its development across different legal 
systems, and the scope and object of Article 150 of the 
Qanun-e-Shahadat Order, 1984. 
 
The Court is mindful that hostility does not always 
reveal itself at the commencement of testimony. A witness 
may 
maintain 
apparent 
consistency 
throughout 
examination-in-chief and yet disclose a fundamentally 
altered position only when subjected to cross-examination. 
Equally, a witness may furnish concessions favourable to


# Page 3

3
the opposite side not because he has abandoned the truth 
but because skillful advocacy has exposed inaccuracies, 
exaggerations, or weaknesses in his account. The law must 
therefore distinguish between a witness who has become 
hostile and a witness who has merely been effectively 
cross-examined. A concession extracted through legitimate 
forensic skill cannot, by itself, justify a declaration of 
hostility. Were the law otherwise, every successful cross-
examination would become a ground for invoking Article 
150. The Court must therefore inquire whether the answer 
given represents a truthful acknowledgment elicited 
through proper questioning or whether it constitutes a 
conscious and unexplained departure from an earlier 
position. The decisive inquiry is not whether the evidence 
has become unfavourable, but whether the witness's 
conduct demonstrates an unwillingness to disclose the 
truth.  
 
 
This distinction finds support in the development 
of the common law itself. The English courts repeatedly 
emphasized that hostility is not established merely because 
a witness gives evidence averse to the party calling him. In 
R v. Figliola [2011 ONCA 457], the Canadian Court of 
Appeal for Ontario recognized that adversity of evidence 
and hostility of disposition are not necessarily synonymous 
concepts. Likewise, under section 3 of the Criminal 
Procedure Act, 1865 (UK), commonly known as Denman's


# Page 4

4
Act, the power to treat a witness as adverse was conferred 
not because his evidence proved unfavourable but because 
his conduct demonstrated a departure from the duty of 
truthful disclosure. The statutory language itself reflected 
the concern that the Court must be satisfied that the witness 
has proved adverse before the exceptional power may be 
invoked. The distinction has remained a constant feature of 
modern evidentiary law.  
 
 
The same principle is discernible in Indian 
jurisprudence. In Sat Paul v. Delhi Administration (AIR 
1976 SC 294), the Supreme Court explained that the grant 
of permission under section 154 of the Indian Evidence Act 
does not rest merely upon the circumstance that the 
testimony has damaged the case of the party calling the 
witness. Rather, the true inquiry concerns the witness's 
attitude, conduct, and veracity, and whether his testimony 
indicates a departure from the obligation of truth. The 
Court observed that the power is intended to serve the 
cause of justice and to enable the Court to reach the truth 
whenever the conduct of the witness renders further testing 
of credibility necessary. The power to permit the cross-
examination of one's own witness is undoubtedly 
discretionary, but that discretion must be exercised upon 
objective considerations. The Court should ordinarily 
examine: (i) whether the witness has materially departed 
from a previous statement on a matter of significance; (ii)


# Page 5

5
whether the departure appears deliberate, evasive, or 
calculated to assist one side of the litigation; (iii) whether 
the altered version has caused genuine surprise to the party 
calling the witness; (iv) whether the inconsistency is 
capable of innocent explanation; and (v) whether the 
interests of justice require further testing of the witness 
through 
questions 
ordinarily 
permissible 
in 
cross-
examination. Hostility is therefore not established merely 
because evidence is unfavourable. Material inconsistency is 
more significant than a minor discrepancy. Concessions 
extracted through legitimate forensic skill do not by 
themselves establish hostility. The Court must determine 
whether the witness has consciously departed from a 
previous position. Where such a departure is established 
and renders further scrutiny necessary in the interests of 
justice and the discovery of truth, the jurisdiction under 
Article 150 becomes available. This approach accords with 
Article 150 of the Qanun-e-Shahadat Order, 1984, whose 
object is not to reward forensic success or compensate for 
evidentiary weakness but to provide the Court with a 
mechanism for testing credibility whenever the conduct of 
the witness threatens the integrity of the fact-finding 
process. 
4.  
The Court would accordingly hold that the 
doctrine of hostile witnesses is not a doctrine about 
procedural stages but a doctrine about the reliability of


# Page 6

6
testimony. Hostility is determined not by the point at which 
it manifests itself but by the conduct through which it is 
revealed. A witness is not hostile because he weakens a 
case; he becomes hostile when he abandons the obligation 
of truth. The power contained in Article 150 is therefore 
available whenever the Court is satisfied that the witness 
has consciously and materially departed from a previous 
position, 
whether 
such 
departure 
emerges 
during 
examination-in-chief, cross-examination, or at any other 
stage of his testimony. The controlling consideration is not 
the convenience of the parties but the Court's enduring duty 
to ensure that justice is founded upon truth rather than 
appearance.  
 
 
This conclusion derives support not only from 
principle but also from comparative authority. Section 154 
of the Indian Evidence Act, 1872, from which Article 150 
of the Qanun-e-Shahadat Order substantially derives its 
language and structure, authorizes the Court, in its 
discretion, to permit the person calling a witness to put any 
questions which might be put in cross-examination by the 
adverse party. Significantly, neither provision imposes any 
temporal restriction upon the exercise of that discretion. 
The absence of such limiting language is itself indicative of 
legislative intent. Had the legislature intended to confine 
the power to examination-in-chief alone, it could easily 
have done so in express terms. Instead, the statutory


# Page 7

7
emphasis is placed upon the satisfaction of the Court and 
not upon the stage of the proceedings. The interpretation 
that the Court is inclined to adopt also finds substantial 
support from Indian jurisprudence, which has examined the 
doctrine with considerable depth. In Sat Paul (supra), the 
Supreme Court of India observed that the power to permit a 
party to cross-examine its own witness is founded upon the 
necessity of discovering the truth and ought not to be 
constrained by technical considerations inherited from the 
older common-law approach. The Court described the 
discretion conferred by section 154 as wide and 
untrammelled and emphasized that the provision exists to 
enable the truthfulness of testimony to be effectively tested 
whenever the conduct of a witness renders such testing 
necessary. In the considered view of this Court, the 
reasoning in Sat Paul supports the broader proposition that 
the doctrine of hostility must be understood as a means of 
protecting the truth-finding process rather than as a 
procedural privilege available only at a predetermined 
stage of examination.  
 
 
A similar principle emerges from Dahyabhai 
Chhaganbhai Thakkar v. State of Gujarat (AIR 1964 SC 
1563), wherein the Supreme Court recognized that the 
statutory power is not confined to any particular stage of 
the examination of a witness and may be exercised 
whenever circumstances justify its invocation. The Court


# Page 8

8
observed, in substance, that the grant of permission 
depends upon the facts and circumstances disclosed before 
the Court and not upon any rigid procedural formula. The 
importance of the decision lies in its rejection of a stage-
centric approach. The Court implicitly acknowledged that 
human conduct cannot be compartmentalized according to 
procedural sequence. A witness may disclose hostility at 
the very outset, during examination-in-chief, during cross-
examination, or even upon further examination. To make 
the availability of judicial power dependent upon the 
fortuitous stage at which such hostility manifests itself 
would be to subordinate substance to form. This Court 
respectfully finds itself in agreement with that reasoning. 
 
 
The same philosophy is reflected in modern 
common-law jurisdictions. Rule 607 of the Federal Rules 
of Evidence of the United States provides that “any party, 
including the party that called the witness, may attack the 
witness’s credibility,” thereby abandoning the historical 
voucher theory and recognizing that credibility is a matter 
for judicial scrutiny whenever circumstances so require. 
Likewise, 
Canadian 
jurisprudence 
has 
consistently 
emphasized that evidentiary rules must be interpreted in a 
manner conducive to the search for truth. In R. v. B. (K.G.) 
[1993] 1 S.C.R. 740, the Supreme Court of Canada 
reiterated that the truth-seeking function of the trial 
constitutes the governing principle of evidentiary law and


# Page 9

9
that procedural doctrines must ultimately serve that 
objective. The comparative jurisprudence, therefore, 
converges upon a single proposition: hostility is a question 
of substance rather than sequence. The jurisdiction to 
invoke Article 150 arises not because a particular stage of 
examination has been reached, but because the conduct of 
the witness has disclosed circumstances rendering the 
ordinary testing of credibility inadequate. Once such 
circumstances appear, whether during examination-in-
chief, cross-examination, re-examination, or at any other 
point before the witness is discharged, the Court remains 
empowered to act. Any contrary interpretation would 
elevate procedural chronology above the administration of 
justice and would frustrate the very purpose for which the 
doctrine of hostile witnesses evolved throughout the 
common-law world.  
5.  
The cumulative effect of these authorities is that 
the doctrine has progressively evolved from a narrow 
procedural exception into a substantive instrument for 
safeguarding the reliability of evidence. As observed by the 
Supreme Court of India in Bhagwan Singh v. State of 
Haryana (1976) 1 SCC 389, the statutory power is intended 
to enable the Court to ascertain the truth when the conduct 
of a witness demonstrates that ordinary examination is no 
longer sufficient for testing the reliability of his testimony. 
Likewise, in Rabindra Kumar Dey v. State of Orissa (1976)


# Page 10

10
4 SCC 233, it was emphasized that hostility is not to be 
inferred merely because evidence becomes unfavourable; 
rather, the Court must examine the attitude of the witness 
and the effect of his testimony upon the truth-finding 
process. What emerges from the jurisprudence is that the 
Court is required to focus not upon the chronology of 
testimony but upon its credibility. The decisive question is 
not when the witness departed from his previous position, 
but whether such departure has in fact occurred and 
whether it is of such a nature as to require further testing 
through the mechanism contemplated by Article 150. The 
Court is, therefore, persuaded that the true ratio flowing 
from these authorities is that hostility is a matter of conduct 
and not of timing. Once a witness demonstrates a conscious 
unwillingness to adhere to a version previously maintained, 
suppresses material facts, or adopts a position indicative of 
evasion or unreliability, the Court's jurisdiction becomes 
available irrespective of the procedural stage at which such 
conduct first becomes apparent. To hold otherwise would 
permit the effectiveness of the law to depend not upon the 
conduct of the witness but upon the order in which 
questions happen to be asked. Such an approach would run 
contrary to the settled principle that procedural rules are 
servants of justice and not its masters. As observed by the 
England & Wales Court of Appeal in Jones v. National 
Coal Board [1957] 2 QB 55, the object of judicial


# Page 11

11
proceedings is to ascertain the truth and to do justice 
according to law; procedural requirements are intended to 
facilitate that objective and not to defeat it.  
6.  
Before adverting to the merits of the controversy, 
it is appropriate to notice the principles governing the 
declaration of a witness as hostile. The Honourable 
Supreme Court in Ghous Baksh v. The State (2025 SCMR 
547) has clarified that mere retraction by a witness from an 
earlier statement does not, by itself, furnish a sufficient 
basis for declaring him hostile. The Court drew a clear 
distinction between retraction and hostility. This distinction 
is deeply rooted in evidentiary jurisprudence. In Syad 
Akbar v. State of Karnataka (AIR 1979 SC 1848), the 
Supreme Court of India observed that inconsistencies in 
testimony differ in nature and significance and that the 
Court must distinguish between ordinary discrepancies 
attributable to human fallibility and departures affecting the 
substance of the case. Retraction may only denote a 
withdrawal from, or inconsistency with, an earlier version, 
whereas hostility imports something more: an attitude 
averse to the party calling the witness, reflected through a 
conscious and material departure from the truth or from the 
case sought to be established by that party. Thus, whether a 
witness has turned hostile is not to be determined 
mechanically or merely on account of some inconsistency, 
but upon an examination of the substance of his testimony


# Page 12

12
as a whole, the nature of the departure made by him, and 
the surrounding circumstances of the case.  
 
 
When the testimony of the witness in the present 
case is examined in the light of the above principle, it 
becomes evident that the matter was not one of a minor 
contradiction, casual omission, or insignificant retraction. 
During his examination-in-chief, the witness supported the 
prosecution's version and specifically implicated the 
accused persons. However, during cross-examination, he 
recanted the most material and incriminating part of his 
earlier testimony, stating that he had not seen the accused 
persons firing. This departure went to the very root of the 
prosecution's case, as it related directly to the identity and 
role of the assailants. It was, therefore, not a deviation on a 
peripheral or collateral aspect, but a substantial retreat from 
the core allegation on which the prosecution's case rested. 
The legal effect of such a material departure was that the 
witness substantially diluted, impaired, and contradicted 
the incriminating force of his examination-in-chief. Such a 
departure strikes at the very rationale underlying Article 
150 of the Qanun-e-Shahadat Order, 1984. The provision is 
not concerned with immaterial discrepancies or ordinary 
failures of recollection; rather, it is directed towards 
situations in which the reliability of testimony itself 
becomes suspect. In such circumstances, the learned trial 
Court was justified in forming the opinion that the witness


# Page 13

13
had adopted a position averse to the prosecution and that it 
was necessary to permit the prosecution to confront him 
with his previous version in terms of Article 150 of the 
Qanun-e-Shahadat Order, 1984. A careful juxtaposition of 
the witness's statement recorded under Section 161 Cr.P.C., 
his examination-in-chief, and his subsequent cross-
examination reveals a discord so pronounced and so 
fundamental that it cannot escape judicial notice. In his 
earlier versions, the statement furnished before the 
Investigating Officer and the testimony tendered in 
examination-in-chief, the witness had spoken in clear, 
categorical, and unequivocal terms. He unreservedly 
implicated the accused persons in the commission of the 
offence and left no room for speculation regarding their 
identity or participation. Significantly, at no stage did he 
allude to darkness, impaired visibility, uncertainty of 
recognition, or any circumstance capable of casting even 
the faintest shadow of doubt upon his ability to identify the 
assailants. His account, as then narrated, was singular in its 
clarity and unwavering in its certainty.  
 
 
Yet, when the crucible of cross-examination 
arrived, the witness sought to clothe himself in an 
altogether different narrative. For the first time, he invoked 
the plea of darkness and attempted to engraft upon the 
record an uncertainty regarding identification a proposition 
wholly alien to his earlier versions and irreconcilable with


# Page 14

14
the certainty he had previously professed. Such a 
transformation is too abrupt to be accidental, too material 
to be inconsequential, and too conveniently timed to be 
dismissed as a mere lapse of memory or an innocuous 
inconsistency. Human recollection may falter on peripheral 
details; it does not ordinarily oscillate so dramatically on 
facts that strike at the very heart of the occurrence. The 
Court cannot remain unmindful of the fact that the witness 
had, by his own earlier statements, foreclosed every avenue 
of doubt concerning the identity of the accused. Having 
once spoken with certainty, his subsequent endeavour to 
resurrect uncertainty appears not as a search for truth but as 
a retreat from it. The change is neither subtle nor 
inadvertent; it bears the unmistakable imprint of a 
conscious and calculated attempt to dilute the incriminating 
force of his earlier testimony and to carve out an 
unwarranted concession in favour of the accused.  
7.  
The attempted departure from truth is so palpable, 
and the shift in stance so deliberate, that it leaves little 
room for any inference other than that the witness 
consciously chose to abandon the path of candor in favour 
of expediency. Courts of law, however, are not passive 
repositories of whatever version a witness may choose to 
advance at different stages of the proceedings. The judicial 
function is not exhausted by the mere recording of 
testimony; it extends to the solemn and often arduous duty


# Page 15

15
of discerning truth amidst contradiction, of separating the 
genuine from the contrived, and of ensuring that justice is 
not sacrificed at the altar of convenience or compromised 
by calculated vacillations. A criminal Court, entrusted as it 
is with the administration of justice, cannot permit the 
sanctity of its process to be undermined by testimony 
fashioned to suit shifting loyalties or changing inclinations. 
It is precisely in the discharge of this solemn duty that the 
learned trial Court exercised its discretion. Far from acting 
precipitately or exceeding the bounds of law, the Court 
below 
displayed 
commendable 
circumspection 
in 
appreciating the witness's conduct in its proper perspective 
and in refusing to accord credence to a version bereft of 
consistency and devoid of the ring of truth. 
 
 
Having 
regard 
to 
the 
entirety 
of 
the 
circumstances, this Court is left with no room for hesitation 
in holding that the witness was rightly declared hostile. 
Indeed, his conduct transcends the technical contours of 
hostility as understood in the law of evidence. The record 
unmistakably reveals a conscious departure from truth, 
animated by an evident desire to shield the accused and to 
frustrate the due course of justice. Testimony so manifestly 
tailored to serve an ulterior purpose forfeits its claim to 
credibility and stands stripped of all evidentiary sanctity. 
The administration of criminal justice cannot be permitted 
to become hostage to witnesses who, having once spoken


# Page 16

16
the truth with certainty, deliberately retreat from it in 
pursuit of a contrary design. Truth may, at times, be 
obscured by hesitation, coloured by loyalty, or clouded by 
expediency; yet it remains the enduring duty of the Court 
to rescue it from such shadows and to restore it to its 
rightful place at the centre of the judicial process. 
8.  
The submission advanced by the learned counsel, 
if 
accepted, 
would 
lead 
to 
consequences 
wholly 
inconsistent with the fair administration of justice. It 
appears difficult to conceive that the law would compel the 
party calling a witness to remain helpless in a situation 
where the witness, while ostensibly supporting that party 
during examination-in-chief, withholds his actual position 
until cross-examination and only then discloses facts 
destructive of the very case he was called to prove. Such an 
approach would place a premium upon calculated 
evasiveness and would enable a witness to play a dual role 
in judicial proceedings. On the one hand, by supporting the 
case during examination-in-chief, he would shield himself 
from the possibility of openly resiling from his previous 
stance; yet, on the other hand, by quietly reserving a 
contradictory version for cross-examination, he could 
inflict substantial damage upon the case of the party who 
produced him, all while depriving that party of any 
meaningful opportunity to confront the deviation at the 
stage when it first emerges. The law of evidence does not


# Page 17

17
countenance such a stratagem. Judicial proceedings are 
intended to facilitate the discovery of truth and not to 
provide a platform for forensic ambushes. This principle 
finds resonance across common-law jurisdictions. Rule 607 
of the Federal Rules of Evidence in the United States rests 
upon the recognition that the search for truth is not served 
by artificial restrictions preventing the effective testing of 
credibility merely because a witness was called by a 
particular party. A witness is expected to speak the truth 
fully and candidly, not to reveal it in fragments according 
to tactical convenience. Where a witness, after lending 
support to a party in examination-in-chief, subsequently 
departs from the most material aspects of that testimony in 
cross-examination, 
thereby 
undermining 
the 
very 
foundation of the case, the Court cannot remain a passive 
spectator. To hold otherwise would render the party calling 
the witness hostage to an unexpected and concealed volte-
face and would permit the truth-finding function of the 
Court to be frustrated by artifice. It is precisely to prevent 
such injustice that the law vests the Court with the 
discretion to permit cross-examination by the party calling 
the witness when his conduct or testimony demonstrates an 
attitude averse to that party's case. The power is not 
intended as a weapon of convenience; rather, it is a 
safeguard against deception, surprise, and unfair prejudice. 
As recognized in Bhagwan Singh (supra), the purpose of


# Page 18

18
the provision is not to repair deficiencies in a party's case 
but to provide the Court with an effective means of testing 
testimony whose reliability has been materially brought 
into question. A witness cannot be permitted to take shelter 
behind a technical distinction and use cross-examination as 
a vehicle for springing a surprise upon the party who called 
him in good faith and with legitimate confidence in the 
truthfulness 
of 
his 
testimony. 
Viewed 
from 
this 
perspective, the declaration of hostility in the present case 
was not merely permissible; it was necessary to preserve 
the fairness and integrity of the trial process. The 
conclusion is consistent with the broader philosophy of 
modern evidentiary law, which regards the search for truth 
as the central function of a judicial proceeding. Article 150 
of the Qanun-e-Shahadat Order, 1984, is one of the 
instruments through which that objective is secured. The 
law cannot allow its procedures to be manipulated in a 
manner that rewards concealment and penalizes trust. Nor 
can it permit a witness, by the timing of his departure from 
a previous position, to determine the availability of a 
jurisdiction which the legislature has deliberately entrusted 
to the judicial discretion of the Court. To hold otherwise 
would be to elevate form over substance and procedural 
sequence over truth itself. 
 
 
The declaration of hostility in the present case, 
therefore, represented not merely a lawful exercise of


# Page 19

19
discretion but a necessary step in preserving the integrity of 
the truth-finding process upon which the administration of 
justice. Consequently, the impugned order reflects a proper 
exercise of judicial discretion. It does not suffer from any 
illegality, material irregularity, or jurisdictional defect 
warranting interference by this Court. The petition is, 
therefore, without merit. 
9.  
There is yet another aspect of the matter which 
deserves notice. The law does not confer the jurisdiction 
embodied in Article 150 merely for the protection of 
litigants. Its broader purpose is to preserve the purity of 
judicial proceedings and to ensure that testimony tendered 
before a Court remains faithful to the truth. A witness who 
enters the witness-box does so under oath and assumes a 
solemn legal obligation to state the truth fully and 
faithfully. The administration of justice necessarily 
proceeds upon the assumption that this obligation will be 
honoured. If a witness were permitted to support one 
version at one stage of the proceedings and thereafter 
consciously depart from it on a material aspect without any 
apprehension of legal consequence, the result would be to 
undermine public confidence in the judicial process itself. 
The Court cannot overlook the practical reality that, in 
some cases, witnesses appear to proceed upon the 
assumption that a material departure from a previous 
version, even if deliberate, carries no consequence beyond


# Page 20

20
the possibility of being treated as hostile. Such an 
assumption finds no support in law. The declaration of 
hostility is not a substitute for accountability. It is merely 
an evidentiary mechanism designed to permit further 
testing of credibility. Where circumstances disclose that a 
witness has knowingly departed from the truth, willfully 
suppressed material facts, or consciously made statements 
inconsistent with what he knows to be true, the matter may 
travel beyond the confines of Article 150 and enter the 
domain of offences relating to false evidence. Sections 191, 
192, and 193 of the Pakistan Penal Code were enacted 
precisely to safeguard the administration of justice from 
such conduct. Those provisions reflect a legislative 
determination that the giving of false evidence in judicial 
proceedings is not a private wrong against a litigant but a 
public wrong against the justice system itself. The object of 
the law is not merely punitive. It is preventative. It seeks to 
ensure that witnesses appreciate that the witness-box is not 
a forum for experimentation, convenience, compromise, or 
tactical adjustment, but a place where the law demands 
complete fidelity to the truth. 
10.  
Comparable concerns have been expressed 
throughout the common-law world. In Chandra Shashi v. 
Anil Kumar Verma (1995) 1 SCC 421, the Supreme Court 
of India observed that the stream of justice must remain 
pure and that those who pollute it by falsehood strike at the


# Page 21

21
very foundation of the judicial process. These observations 
resonate with equal force in the context of our legal system. 
This Court would therefore observe that where a trial Court 
forms the view, on a tentative assessment of the material 
before it, that a witness has consciously and deliberately 
departed from the truth on a matter of substance, the Court 
should not regard the declaration of hostility as necessarily 
exhausting the available judicial response. Subject always 
to the safeguards provided by law and the particular facts 
of the case, consideration may also be given to the 
provisions relating to false evidence and offences affecting 
the administration of justice. The consistent and principled 
application of those provisions serves an important public 
purpose. It discourages deliberate falsehood, reinforces 
respect for the oath administered by the Court, and ensures 
that witnesses do not proceed upon the mistaken belief that 
calculated departures from the truth are devoid of legal 
consequence.
