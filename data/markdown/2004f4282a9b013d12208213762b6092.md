                           J U D G M E N T 
Dr. Khurshid Iqbal, J.-  
1.  
In the subject constitutional petitions, the grievance of all the 
petitioners is that the Lower Dir’s District Police Officer (respondent No. 1) 
has posted their names and pictures on social media platforms, such as, 
Facebook, tagging them as touts, land grabbers, qabza mafia, and drug 
peddlers. They contend that such information is false, without lawful 
justification, illegal, and in utter violation of their constitutionally guaranteed 
fundamental rights. Claiming to be law-abiding and peaceful citizens who 
believe in the supremacy of the law and the Constitution, the petitioners pray 
for a declaration that the published information in question is false, unlawful, 
and unconstitutional. They further pray for directions to respondent No. 1 
to: (a) stop publishing such information on social media platforms; (b) 
immediately remove it from the official Facebook account of the District 
Police; (c) refrain from making raids on their houses and harassing them on 
the basis of such information; and (d) respect their fundamental rights; 
alongside any other relief this Court may deem appropriate.

2.  
Today, the learned Additional Advocate General (A.A.G.), along 
with a departmental representative of the police department, appeared 
before the Court on behalf of the respondents. At the very outset, the learned 
A.A.G. stated at the bar that respondent No. 1 (the concerned DPO) has 
already been transferred from the District. He added that the information in 
question has been removed from the social media platforms and is no longer 
available. 
 
3.  
Be that as it may, this Court inquired from the learned A.A.G. under 
what law and policy the information in question had been posted on social 
media platforms. The learned A.A.G. could not furnish a satisfactory reply to 
the Court’s query. Instead, he termed it a factual controversy, attempting to 
question the maintainability of these petitions under the constitutional 
jurisdiction of this Court. This objection is misconceived. Where executive 
action is taken wholly without lawful authority and operates to the 
detriment of the fundamental rights of citizens, the jurisdiction of this Court 
under Article 199 of the Constitution is squarely attracted, and the mere 
characterisation of such action as a factual controversy does not oust it, 
particularly where the foundational fact, namely the posting of the impugned 
material, has not been denied.1 
 
4.  
It is legally well-settled that no state institution, including most 
notably the police, has any legal authority to brand a citizen as a criminal 
without lawful justification and in violation of the due process of law. Official 
machinery cannot be employed to stigmatise citizens without lawful 
authority. The launching of such platforms to brandish individuals as touts, 
land grabbers, or qabza mafia without any statutory backing constitutes a 
serious abuse of official authority. The State and its functionaries are creatures 
of the law and may exercise only such powers as the law confers upon them. 
No provision of law empowers the police to constitute itself a forum of 
public condemnation, or to publish, in anticipation of trial, lists branding 
citizens as criminals. To permit the executive to pronounce guilt through the 
medium of social media would be to substitute the assertion of the accuser 
for the verdict of the Court, a course wholly alien to the constitutional 
scheme.2 
 
5.  
Such actions directly violate the Presumption of Innocence3, a 
cornerstone of criminal jurisprudence explicitly protected under the right to

and labelling citizens on social media platforms before a formal conviction 
by a competent court constitutes an irreversible onslaught on the Inviolability 
of Dignity of Man and the Privacy of Home (Article 14 of the Constitution)4. 
No state organ has the licence to publicise defamatory lists that damage a 
citizen's reputation, as the right to life includes the right to a clean and 
unblemished name.5 These guarantees are not ornamental. They impose a 
positive obligation upon every organ of the State to refrain from conduct 
that pre-empts the verdict of a Court of competent jurisdiction, and to ensure 
that the coercive and communicative power of the State is not turned against 
the dignity of the very citizens it exists to protect. In the digital environment, 
particularly on an official social-media page, such publication is not a passing 
administrative notation; it is a State-authored stigma capable of instant 
circulation, indefinite retention, and repeated republication. The absence of 
a clear statutory warrant is therefore fatal, for executive convenience cannot 
be permitted to convert accusation into public condemnation. The legal 
injury is aggravated, rather than diluted, by the digital forum: social-media 
communication may reach an audience far beyond the immediate 
community, may be recirculated by strangers, and, once replicated, may 
escape effective recall.6

7.  
It is clear from the above that the initial action of respondent No. 
1 was entirely without lawful justification, statutory backing, or policy. The 
posting of the information has not been explicitly denied. However, since 
the names of the petitioners have now been removed from the social media 
platforms, this Court sees no need for further proceedings on that specific 
aspect. The subsequent removal of the offending material does not, 
however, render the controversy as academic. The legality of the impugned 
executive action remains a matter of public importance, and a declaration is 
necessary both to vindicate the fundamental rights that were infringed and 
to guard against any recurrence of like conduct. 
 
8.  
Consequently, this Court declares the impugned action of posting 
such defamatory information on social media as against the law and the 
Constitution, that is to say, to have been taken without lawful authority and 
to be of no legal effect within the contemplation of Article 199 of the 
Constitution, and to be violative of the fundamental rights guaranteed under 
Articles 4, 9, 10-A and 14 thereof, and strictly directs the respondents not to 
harass the petitioners solely on the basis of the information in question. This 
Court further directs the worthy Inspector General of Police, Khyber 
Pakhtunkhwa, to conduct an impartial inquiry into the matter. The inquiry 
shall, in particular, ascertain the authority, instruction or policy, if any, under 
which the impugned material came to be posted, and shall determine and fix 
responsibility therefor. If the allegations levelled by the petitioners regarding 
unauthorised and malicious posting are proved, respondent No. 1 shall be 
proceeded against in accordance with the law. The inquiry shall be 
completed within two months from the date of receipt of a copy of this 
judgment, and a copy of its report shall be sent to the Additional Registrar 
of this Court for perusal by this Court in Chamber. These petitions are 
disposed of accordingly.
