It has been contended that the services of the petitioner

6. 
We have heard learned counsel for the parties and 
learned DAG for the respondents at considerable length 
and have gone through the record with their able 
assistance. 
7.    Admittedly, the petitioner was not appointed against 
a regular sanctioned post through a regular recruitment 
process. The appointment letter as well as subsequent 
extension orders appended with the petition clearly 
demonstrate that the petitioner’s engagement was purely 
temporary and contractual/daily wage in nature. The 
terms and conditions embodied therein specifically 
provided that his services were liable to termination at any 
time without notice and without assigning any reason. 
Such stipulation leaves little room for creation of any 
enforceable vested right in favour of the petitioner. The law 
is now fairly settled that employees engaged on daily 
wages, contingent basis, work-charge basis or temporary 
arrangements do not acquire indefeasible right either to 
continuation in service or to regularization merely on the 
basis of length of service.  
8. 
In the instant case, no material has been brought 
on record to establish the existence of any statutory 
provision, notified policy, or legal framework under which 
the petitioner could legitimately claim regularization as a 
matter of right. On the contrary, the record reflects that 
the petitioner remained engaged throughout on a purely 
temporary basis as a daily-wage employee receiving fixed 
monthly wages. Such an arrangement, by its very nature, 
does not confer any vested or enforceable right to seek 
regular appointment or continuation in service. It is also 
pertinent to observe that the relevant statutory framework

4 | P a g e 
 
excludes from the ambit of regularization those employees 
who are appointed against project posts, work-charge 
establishments, or are paid from contingencies. The 
legislative intent behind such exclusion is manifest and 
unambiguous, that the benefit of regularization is 
confined to employees appointed on adhoc or contractual 
basis under the prescribed scheme, and not to those 
engaged on daily-wages or work-charge arrangements, 
which are inherently temporary, non-pensionable, and 
contingent upon the continuation of specific projects, 
schemes, or availability of funds.  
9. 
The record further reveals that the petitioner was 
engaged under temporary and time-bound arrangements, 
which were extended from time to time subject to 
administrative necessity, availability of posts, and 
financial resources. However, such extensions cannot, by 
any stretch of interpretation, convert a temporary 
engagement 
into 
permanent 
employment. 
The 
respondents have specifically pleaded that the services of 
the petitioner, along with other similarly situated 
employees, were discontinued on account of financial 
constraints, HR rationalization, and non-availability of 
sanctioned posts. In the absence of any statutory 
protection or vested right in favour of the petitioner, the 
discontinuation of his temporary engagement cannot be 
termed unlawful. 
10. 
The administrative authorities are the proper forum 
to 
determine 
matters 
relating 
to 
organizational 
requirements, 
staffing 
patterns, 
and 
financial 
sustainability, keeping in view the institutional needs and 
available fiscal resources. In exercise of constitutional 
jurisdiction, this Court cannot sit as an appellate 
authority over administrative decisions nor substitute its 
own wisdom for that of the competent authorities unless 
the impugned action is shown to be tainted with mala fide, 
arbitrariness, discrimination, or patent illegality. It is 
further well-settled that the question of regularization 
squarely falls within the domain of policy-making and 
remains an exclusive prerogative of the Executive. Such 
matters ordinarily do not invite judicial interference, 
particularly where no statutory scheme or declared policy 
conferring a right of regularization has been brought on 
record. Guidance in this regard may be sought from the 
judgment of the Honorable Supreme Court of Pakistan in

Another significant aspect of the matter is that 
pursuant to directions of this Court passed in W.P. 
No.2750-P/2025,

No.02 
duly

thereafter passed a speaking order after considering the 
stance of both sides. The impugned order does not suffer 
from any jurisdictional defect, procedural impropriety, 
violation of natural justice or patent illegality warranting 
interference by this Court in exercise of constitutional 
jurisdiction. It is trite law that constitutional jurisdiction 
is discretionary and equitable in nature. Such jurisdiction 
is not ordinarily invoked to enforce contractual or 
temporary engagements particularly where no statutory 
right is shown to have been infringed. The petitioner has 
failed to establish any vested legal right capable of 
enforcement through issuance of constitutional writ.  
14. 
For what has been discussed above, this Court is of 
the considered view that the petitioner has not been able 
to make out a case for interference. The impugned order 
dated 11.07.2025, passed by respondent No.02 is found 
to be lawful, reasoned and in accordance with settled 
principles of law. Consequently, the instant writ petition 
being devoid of merit is hereby dismissed.
