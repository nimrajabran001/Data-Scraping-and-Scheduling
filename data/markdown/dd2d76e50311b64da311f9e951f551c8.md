3. Heard. Record perused. 
4. As per contents of the FIR, on receipt of a public 
complaint regarding alleged use of a rented house situated 
at Tanki Mor, Mansehra for prostitution, the local police 
conducted a raid whereupon petitioner Madiha Bibi along 
with petitioner Noor Muhammad were allegedly found in 
one room, whereas petitioner Asima Bibi along with one 
Saifullah, who allegedly escaped from the spot, were found 
in another room. 
5. Significantly, there is no allegation in the FIR that the 
present petitioners or any other person was engaged in 
buying, selling, trafficking, hiring or commercial dealing 
of any human being for purposes of prostitution. There is 
also no allegation that any money was recovered, any 
customer was found negotiating such transaction, or that 
any material indicative of organized prostitution or 
trafficking was secured during the raid. 
6. Sections 371-A and 371-B PPC are intended to curb 
trafficking, sale, purchase and commercial exploitation of 
human beings for purposes of prostitution. Mere allegation

of immoral conduct or mere presence of a male and female 
inside a private premises does not ipso facto attract the said 
provisions unless material exists showing buying, selling, 
hiring, trafficking or commercial sexual exploitation of a 
person. Reference in this regard may be made to Khalida 
Bibi and another v. The State and others (2024 P Cr. LJ 
1972), Bilal Jan v. The State (2021 P Cr. LJ 323), Mst. 
Kainat v. The State (2016 YLR 974) and Shafqat Ali v. The 
State (2009 YLR 60). 
7. The contents of the FIR, even if accepted at their face 
value, do not prima facie disclose the essential ingredients 
of sections 371-A or 371-B PPC. At best, the allegations 
merely suggest presence of opposite genders inside a 
private house. Such circumstance by itself does not 
constitute the offences alleged. In Khalida Bibi and 
another v. The State and others (2024 P Cr. LJ 1972), it 
was specifically held that even where a male and female 
are found in an objectionable condition, sections 371-A 
and 371-B PPC are not automatically attracted unless 
material exists showing sale, purchase or hiring of a person 
for prostitution. 
8. The manner in which the raid was conducted also 
raises 
serious 
legal 
and 
constitutional 
concerns. 
Admittedly, the raid was carried out at a private dwelling

house without obtaining any search warrant from a 
Magistrate and without associating respectable persons of 
the locality. Such intrusion into a private home, 
particularly involving arrest of women, directly touches 
upon the constitutional guarantees relating to dignity of 
man, privacy of home and liberty protected under Articles 
4, 9 and 14 of the Constitution. In Salman Ghazanfar v. 
The State (2018 MLD 1446), this Court observed that raid 
at a private premises without obtaining search warrant and 
without compliance of section 103 Cr.P.C. amounted to 
unlawful intrusion prohibited by law. Similar view was 
expressed in Mst. Kainat v. The State (2016 YLR 974) and 
Mohabat v. State (2019 YLRN 51 Karachi). 
9. Courts have repeatedly cautioned that under the garb 
of offences relating to prostitution and immorality, 
constitutional protections available to citizens cannot be 
permitted to be violated through arbitrary police action 
founded merely upon suspicion, anonymous complaints or 
spy information. In Mohabat v. State (2019 YLRN 51 
Karachi), it was observed that no police official should be 
allowed immunity for degrading dignity and reputation of 
citizens through unlawful intrusion into private life. 
10. Even otherwise, where allegations merely relate to 
consensual intercourse or fornication, prosecution, if any,
