8. 
We have carefully examined the said reliance. 
With utmost respect, the judgment does not lay down 
any such broad or inflexible proposition. The Apex 
Court was not enunciating a rule of universal exclusion 
of an FIR merely on the ground that it emanates from 
the accused. On the contrary, it undertook a cautious 
and well-reasoned examination of the evidentiary 
character of such an FIR and held that only those 
portions which are in the nature of confessional or 
otherwise legally inadmissible incriminations are to be 
excluded from consideration, whereas the remaining 
admissible facts, if any, may legitimately be read in


# Page 10

10
conjunction with the rest of the prosecution evidence. 
Significantly, the conclusion reached by the Apex Court 
in the cited case was not founded solely upon the 
exclusion of the FIR. The Court proceeded further to re-
evaluate the entire prosecution case on the basis of the 
remaining evidence and, upon such holistic assessment, 
found the prosecution version to be doubtful, thereby 
extending the benefit of doubt to the accused. Thus, the 
true ratio decidendi of the said judgment is not that an 
FIR lodged by an accused must be discarded in its 
entirety, but rather that its admissibility is subject to 
well-settled principles of criminal jurisprudence, and its 
probative value must be determined after segregating 
the inadmissible portions from the admissible ones, in 
the context of the entire evidentiary record. Viewed in 
this perspective, the reliance placed by the learned 
counsel, though ingenious, is clearly distinguishable on 
facts and does not support the sweeping exclusion of the 
FIR as contended. The judgment of the Apex Court, 
when read in its proper legal and factual context, leaves 
no ambiguity that the ultimate determination always 
rests upon the totality of the admissible evidence, and 
not upon any isolated exclusion of the FIR in toto.

The crucial question, however, is whether the exclusion 
of the confessional element necessarily renders the 
entire report devoid of legal effect, or whether the non-
confessional and otherwise relevant portions of the 
report continue to remain admissible and capable of 
being taken into account. It is this delicate legal 
distinction that the Court is required to examine before 
evaluating the remaining prosecution evidence and 
determining whether the conviction recorded by the 
learned Trial Court can be sustained. 
9. 
In order to determine the legal effect and 
evidentiary value of the FIR lodged by the appellant 
himself, it is necessary to advert to the relevant 
provisions of the Qanun-e-Shahadat Order, 1984, which 
are couched in clear, mandatory, and unambiguous 
language. Article 38 of the Qanun-e-Shahadat Order 
categorically provides: "No confession made to a police 
officer shall be proved as against a person accused of 
any offence." Likewise, Article 39 stipulates: "Subject 
to Article 40, no confession made by any person whilst 
he is in the custody of a police officer, unless it be made 
in the immediate presence of a Magistrate, shall be 
proved as against such person." The wisdom 
underlying these provisions is deeply rooted in the 
principle that no person can be compelled to furnish 
evidence against himself and that confessions made


# Page 12

12
before police officials are inherently susceptible to 
abuse, coercion, and manipulation. Consequently, the 
legislature has consciously deprived such statements of 
evidentiary value except to the limited extent expressly 
recognized by law. We are, therefore, of the considered 
view that the portion of the FIR wherein the appellant 
purportedly admitted having caused the death of the 
deceased and disclosed the motive behind the 
occurrence is, in substance, nothing but a confession 
made before a police officer. Such a statement squarely 
attracts the mischief contemplated by Articles 38 and 39 
of the Qanun-e-Shahadat Order and cannot be treated as 
substantive evidence against its maker. Its legal status is 
not different from any other incriminating statement 
allegedly made by an accused before a police officer 
during the course of investigation. The law does not 
permit such a statement to be used as proof of guilt 
merely because it was incorporated in the form of a 
First Information Report. However, the matter does not 
end there. The exclusion of the confessional part of the 
report does not necessarily render the entire FIR 
inadmissible. Article 40 of the Qanun-e-Shahadat Order 
embodies a limited exception to the rule of exclusion 
and permits proof of only that much information 
supplied by an accused as distinctly relates to a fact 
thereby discovered. Similarly, judicial precedent has


# Page 13

13
consistently recognized that where an FIR is lodged by 
the accused himself, only the incriminatory or 
confessional portion thereof is liable to be excluded, 
whereas the remaining non-confessional part may still 
be looked into for such purposes as are legally 
permissible.  
 
The august Supreme Court of Pakistan in PLD 
1965 SC 366 (Muhammad Saleh v. The State) held that a 
confessional statement embodied in an FIR lodged by 
the accused cannot be used against him because of its 
confessional character. Likewise, in PLD 1956 SC 420 
(Muhammad Bakhsh v. The State), it was observed that 
an FIR made by the accused himself is liable to 
exclusion to the extent that it contains admissions of 
guilt. Furthermore, in Mohabat v. The State (1975 
PCr.L.J. 882), it was held that where an FIR is lodged 
by the accused, the incriminating part alone is 
inadmissible while the remaining portion may still be 
considered subject to the rules of evidence.  
10. 
The principle is not peculiar to our jurisdiction. 
Comparable provisions contained in Section 25 of the 
Indian Evidence Act, 1872, have consistently been 
interpreted by the superior courts in India to mean that a 
confession made before a police officer is wholly 
inadmissible, except to the limited extent permitted


# Page 14

14
under the discovery rule embodied in Section 27 of the 
said Act. The rationale underlying both statutory 
regimes remains the same: to guard against the danger 
of convictions based on unverified confessions made 
before police authorities. Applying the above principles 
to the facts of the present case, we have no hesitation in 
holding that the appellant's admission regarding the 
commission of the homicide and the motive attributed 
thereto cannot be read in evidence as proof of guilt. 
Nevertheless, the prosecution is not rendered remediless 
merely because the confessional part of the FIR stands 
excluded. The Court is still required to examine whether 
the remaining circumstances, including the recoveries 
effected on the pointation of the appellant, the discovery 
of the dead body, the medical evidence, and other 
independent incriminating circumstances, are sufficient 
to sustain the conviction recorded by the learned Trial 
Court.

recovered the dead body of the deceased from a drum

recovery was not an isolated circumstance; rather, it 
was accompanied by the recovery of blood-stained

well-settled that the discovery of a fact pursuant to 
information supplied by an accused, to the extent it 
leads to recovery of a material object, is a relevant 
circumstance under the law of evidence. When these 
circumstances are examined in their totality, namely, 
the admitted matrimonial relationship, the undisputed 
ownership and control of the house, the recovery of the 
dead body from a concealed place within that house, the 
presence of blood-stained material and weapon of 
offence, and the conduct of the appellant in leading the 
police to the place of recovery, they collectively form a 
coherent chain of circumstances. While no single 
circumstance, in isolation, may be conclusive of guilt, 
their cumulative effect cannot be ignored. At the same 
time, this Court is conscious of the well-settled

principle that suspicion, however strong, cannot take the 
place of proof, and that in cases resting on 
circumstantial evidence, the prosecution must establish 
a chain so complete as to exclude every reasonable 
hypothesis consistent with the innocence of the accused. 
It is, therefore, in this broader legal framework that the 
remaining evidence must be carefully evaluated to 
determine whether the inference of guilt is the only 
reasonable conclusion that can be drawn from the facts.  
16. 
The argument that the accused happens to be the 
husband of the deceased and the father of minor 
children cannot, by itself, furnish a lawful ground for 
acquittal. Are we to become oblivious to the demands of 
justice merely because the conviction of the accused 
may leave his children without the immediate care and 
protection of a parent? If such a consideration alone 
were permitted to prevail, would it not amount to a 
grave miscarriage of justice? These are consequences 
which ought to have weighed with the accused at the 
time of committing the offence. The law cannot be 
rendered subservient to considerations of sympathy 
which arise only after the commission of a crime. 
Courts are guardians of justice, not dispensers of 
misplaced compassion. While the welfare of innocent 
children is undoubtedly a matter of concern and 
deserves every possible protection from the family and

28
society, such considerations cannot eclipse the rights of 
the victim nor dilute the accountability of an offender. 
To hold otherwise would create an impermissible 
precedent whereby the existence of dependents becomes 
a shield against criminal responsibility. Comparative 
jurisprudence 
likewise 
recognizes 
that 
personal 
hardships flowing from a conviction, including the 
impact upon an offender’s family, cannot ordinarily 
outweigh the administration of justice. Courts have 
consistently maintained that criminal liability is 
determined by evidence and law, not by the collateral 
consequences that may befall the offender’s relatives. 
The hardship suffered by family members, however 
unfortunate, is a consequence of the offender’s own 
conduct and not a factor capable of absolving him from 
legal responsibility. The children of the deceased and 
the accused are undoubtedly innocent sufferers of the 
tragedy; however, the Court cannot permit their 
unfortunate circumstances to obscure the central 
question 
requiring 
determination 
whether 
the 
prosecution has succeeded in proving the guilt of the 
accused in accordance with law. Once the evidence 
establishes culpability, the Court would be failing in its 
solemn duty if it were to extend the benefit of acquittal 
merely on the ground that the accused has minor 
children who may be deprived of his company or care.

17. 
The attending circumstances of the present case 
clearly establish that the prosecution has succeeded in
