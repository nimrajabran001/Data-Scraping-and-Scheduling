allowed to influence the judicial mind. Courts 
of law do not dispense justice on the basis of 
emotion, outrage, public sentiment, or the 
perceived abhorrence of an accusation. Their 
legitimacy 
rests 
upon 
their 
unwavering 
commitment to impartiality, objectivity, and 
adherence to the rule of law. It is often said 
that justice is blind. The principle signifies that 
courts must remain indifferent to passion and 
prejudice and must evaluate every case solely 
on the touchstone of evidence and law. The 
more serious the accusation, the greater 
becomes the obligation upon the Court to


# Page 22

22
guard 
itself 
against 
assumptions 
and 
preconceived notions. Indeed, if the gravity of 
an incident or its capacity to shock society 
were permitted to influence the judicial 
process, it would become exceedingly difficult 
to ensure the fair administration of justice. In 
such circumstances, the right to a fair trial, one 
of 
the 
most 
fundamental 
guarantees 
recognized by civilized legal systems, would 
stand compromised. The consequences of 
such an approach would be grave. A 
conviction founded upon emotion rather than 
evidence would not constitute justice; it would 
amount to a miscarriage thereof. Such a 
course would offend not only the principles of 
criminal jurisprudence but also the dictates of 
morality and the teachings of religion itself, all 
of which insist that a person be judged fairly 
and only upon proof that satisfies the requisite 
legal standard. The administration of criminal 
justice can neither permit nor afford such an 
eventuality. It is for this reason that every 
accused person, regardless of the nature of 
the allegations levelled against him, remains


# Page 23

23
entitled to have his case examined with 
complete fairness, to have the evidence 
against him scrutinized with care, and to have 
every doubt and inconsistency tested in 
accordance with law. The burden always rests 
upon the prosecution to establish its case 
beyond reasonable doubt, and unless that 
burden is discharged, no conviction can 
lawfully 
be 
sustained. 
Therefore, 
before 
expressing 
any 
opinion 
regarding 
the 
culpability or innocence of the appellant, and 
before 
determining 
whether 
the 
findings 
recorded by the learned trial Court are 
sustainable, this Court deems it desirable to 
take 
into

the

the manner in which the same has been 
appreciated by the Court below.

Liability

The
