    JUDGMENT 
 
MUHAMMAD NAEEM ANWAR, J: - This civil revision is 
directed 
against 
the 
judgment 
and 
order 
dated 07.05.2026 passed by the learned Additional District 
Judge-III, Peshawar, whereby the appeal preferred by 
respondent No.1 against the order dated 16.10.2025 of the 
learned Civil Judge-VIII, Peshawar was accepted. Through the 
impugned appellate judgment, the order of the trial Court 
allowing the petitioner's application under Order VII Rule 11 of 
the Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 (CPC) and rejecting the plaint 
was set aside. The matter was remanded to the trial Court for 
decision on merits after recording evidence. Simultaneously, the 
application filed by the petitioner under Order VII Rule 11 CPC 
was dismissed with costs of Rs. 50,000/-. 
2. 
Learned counsel for petitioners were heard at 
considerable length and the available record has been examined. 
3. 
The record reveals that respondent No.1, Mst. Rukhsana, 
instituted a suit for recovery of Rs. 14,246,722/- on account of 
various claims including arrears of rent, electricity charges, gas 
dues, maintenance expenses, penalty imposed by the Peshawar 
Development Authority, trade licence fees and damages. The 
foundation of the suit was that she is owner of House No. 31-G, 
Street No.2, Sector N-4, Phase-IV, Hayatabad, Peshawar, 
measuring fifteen marlas, which had been let out to the

petitioner. According to the plaint, the petitioner committed 
default in payment of rent, surrendered possession on 
25.06.2020 and left the premises in a severely damaged 
condition. It was further alleged that after taking possession she 
discovered 
notices 
issued 
by 
the 
Town 
Municipal 
Administration and the Excise and Taxation Department 
regarding trade licence fees and other liabilities, besides an Iqrar 
Nama allegedly 
demonstrating 
that 
the

had 
transferred possession of the premises to his brother who was 
using it as a guest house. On these allegations, recovery of the 
aforesaid amount was sought. The petitioner entered 
appearance and filed written statement contesting the suit on 
numerous legal and factual grounds. Alongside the written 
statement, an application under Order VII Rule 11 CPC was 
moved seeking rejection of the plaint. The learned trial Court 
accepted the application and rejected the plaint, which order was 
reversed by the appellate Court. 
4. 
During the course of arguments, learned counsel for the 
petitioner was specifically confronted with the contents of the 
application under Order VII Rule 11 CPC and was asked to 
identify the statutory ground on which rejection of the plaint 
could legitimately be sustained. A careful reading of the 
application demonstrates that none of the objections raised 
therein correspond to any of the contingencies enumerated in 
Order VII Rule 11 CPC. The objections essentially constitute 
defences to the plaintiff's claim and are matters requiring 
adjudication after framing of issues and recording of evidence. 
More recently, in Misree Khan and others vs. Abdul Ghafoor 
and others (PLD 2025 SC 24), the august Supreme Court 
reiterated that rejection of a plaint is a drastic jurisdiction which 
deprives a litigant of the opportunity to substantiate his claim 
through evidence. Consequently, where adjudication of the 
controversy requires recording of evidence or determination of 
disputed questions of fact, rejection of the plaint would be wholly 
unwarranted. Such controversies must be resolved through a


# Page 3

3 
 
 
regular trial and not by summarily terminating the proceedings 
at the threshold. 
5. 
Order VII Rule 11 CPC embodies a summary 
jurisdiction of an exceptional nature. It authorizes rejection of a 
plaint only where one or more of the statutory conditions 
expressly mentioned therein exist. While exercising such 
jurisdiction, the Court is confined to the averments contained in 
the plaint and the documents accompanying it and is not 
permitted to embark upon an enquiry into the correctness, 
truthfulness or otherwise of the disputed facts pleaded by the 
parties. 
Questions 
involving 
appreciation 
of 
evidence, 
examination of rival claims or determination of disputed rights 
fall outside the scope of Order VII Rule 11 CPC. The settled 
principle is that where determination of the controversy requires 
investigation into disputed facts, appreciation of evidence or 
resolution of complicated questions of fact intertwined with law, 
the plaint cannot be rejected summarily and the matter must 
proceed to trial. The object of the provision is to terminate 
incompetent litigation at its inception, but not to deny a litigant 
the opportunity of establishing a cause through evidence where 
the plaint, on its face, discloses a triable issue.  
6. 
The august Supreme Court in Raja Ali Shan vs. Messrs 
Essem Hotel Limited and others (2007 SCMR 741) 
authoritatively held that the Court possesses independent 
jurisdiction to reject a plaint, even in the absence of an 
application by the defendant, if the case squarely falls within the 
parameters of Order VII Rule 11 CPC. Equally, where the 
statutory conditions are absent, neither the application of a party 
nor the Court's discretion can be invoked to reject the plaint. 
Likewise, in Muhammad Zaman and others vs. Shah Wazir 
Khan (PLD 2002 Peshawar 45), it was observed that an 
incompetent lis deserves burial at its inception. However, the 
principle applies only where the incompetency is apparent on 
the face of the plaint itself. It does not extend to cases where the

4 
 
 
alleged incompetency depends upon disputed facts or matters 
requiring evidence. In the present case, the petitioner sought 
rejection of the plaint on grounds which are essentially matters 
of defence. Whether rent remained unpaid; whether the 
petitioner was responsible for electricity and gas dues; whether 
the premises were converted into a guest house; whether damage 
was caused to the property; whether the alleged liabilities are 
legally recoverable; and whether the plaintiff is entitled to 
damages are all questions which cannot be adjudicated without 
recording evidence. These are neither admitted facts nor legal 
bars apparent from the plaint itself. Their determination 
necessarily requires framing of issues, production of 
documentary evidence and examination of witnesses. Such 
disputed questions cannot be resolved within the narrow 
compass of Order VII Rule 11 CPC. 
7. 
It is equally well settled that rejection of a plaint and 
dismissal of a suit are two distinct legal concepts operating in 
different spheres. The former relates to the maintainability of 
the plaint itself, whereas the latter follows adjudication on merits 
after trial. These two concepts are neither interchangeable nor 
capable of being merged. Rejection of a plaint under Order VII 
Rule 11 CPC merely terminates the plaint and, subject to the 
provisions of law, leaves intact the plaintiff's right to institute a 
fresh suit under Order VII Rule 13 CPC where permissible. 
Conversely, dismissal of a suit is a decision on the rights of the 
parties after adjudication and ordinarily operates as a bar in 
accordance with law. This distinction has been lucidly explained 
by the Honourable Supreme Court in Shaheem Nasir Khan vs. 
Mst. Asmat Ara and others (PLD 2002 Karachi 408). The 
objections raised by the petitioner may ultimately constitute valid 
grounds for dismissal of the suit after recording evidence, but 
they cannot furnish a lawful basis for rejection of the plaint. The 
learned appellate Court, therefore, rightly concluded that the 
learned trial Court travelled beyond the limited jurisdiction

conferred by Order VII Rule 11 CPC and correctly remanded 
the matter for decision on merits after recording evidence. 
8. 
The only part of the impugned judgment warranting 
interference relates to the award of costs amounting to Rs. 
50,000/-The Code of Civil Procedure draws a clear distinction 
between ordinary costs under Section 35, C.P.C. and 
compensatory costs under Section 35-A, C.P.C. While Section 
35 confers a discretionary power upon the Court to award 
ordinary costs of litigation with a view to indemnifying the 
successful party for expenses reasonably incurred in prosecuting 
or defending the proceedings, Section 35-A creates a special and 
penal jurisdiction enabling the Court to award compensatory 
costs only where the claim or defence is found to be false or 
vexatious to the knowledge of the party raising it and the statutory 
conditions prescribed therein have been strictly fulfilled. More 
importantly, the jurisdiction to award compensatory costs under 
Section 35-A, C.P.C. is vested exclusively in the Court of first 
instance trying the suit. The appellate Court does not possess an 
independent or concurrent jurisdiction to exercise that power. 
This legal position is placed beyond doubt by the proviso to 
Order XLI, Rule 33, C.P.C., which expressly restrains an 
appellate Court from making any order under Section 35-A in 
respect of an objection upon which the trial Court has omitted 
or refused to pass such an order. Although Rule 33 confers wide 
discretionary powers upon an appellate Court to pass any decree 
or order which ought to have been passed by the Court below, 
the proviso engrafts a specific statutory limitation by excluding 
the jurisdiction to award compensatory costs under Section 35-
A, C.P.C. Consequently, while an appellate Court may affirm, 
modify or set aside ordinary costs awarded under Section 35, 
C.P.C., it is denuded of jurisdiction to independently impose 
compensatory costs. This principle has been authoritatively 
affirmed in Sindh Industrial Trading Estates through its 
Secretary, Karachi Vs. Mst. Qamar Hilal and 5 others (2001 
SCMR 1680) and reiterated in S.I.T.E. vs. Mst. Qamar Hilal

and others (2002 MLD 1569), wherein it was held that the 
special jurisdiction conferred by Section 35-A, C.P.C. is 
exercisable only by the trial Court and cannot be assumed by the 
appellate Court in view of the express bar contained in the 
proviso to Order XLI, Rule 33, C.P.C. Reliance may be placed 
on the principle enunciated in the cases of Abdul Rahim Sathi 
vs. Ghulam Sarwar and 11 other (2009 CLC 1034), Mir Nawaz 
Khan vs. Gul Ayub Khan and another (2003 CLC 1428), Asghar 
Ali Khan vs. Mst. Feroza Begum and others (2004 YLR 1066), 
Muhammad Zia vs. Ch. Nazir Muhamad Advocate and others 
(2002 CLC 59).  
9. 
In the present case, the learned appellate Court rightly 
concluded that the plaint could not have been rejected under 
Order VII, Rule 11, C.P.C., as the controversy between the 
parties involved disputed and intricate questions of fact and law 
requiring determination after framing of issues and recording of 
evidence. The order of remand, therefore, suffers from no legal 
infirmity and calls for no interference. However, while allowing 
the appeal, the learned appellate Court further burdened the 
petitioner with costs of Rs. 50,000/-. The nature and tenor of the 
order unmistakably demonstrate that the amount was imposed 
as compensatory costs and not as ordinary costs under Section 
35, C.P.C. Such an exercise of jurisdiction is expressly 
prohibited by the proviso to Order XLI, Rule 33, C.P.C. and is, 
therefore, without lawful authority. Accordingly, this civil 
revision fails on merits insofar as the remand of the suit for trial 
is concerned, as the learned appellate Court rightly held that the 
controversy could not be adjudicated within the limited scope of 
Order VII, Rule 11, C.P.C. and necessarily required a full-
fledged trial. However, the direction imposing costs of Rs. 
50,000/- being beyond the jurisdiction vested in the appellate 
Court under Section 35-A read with the proviso to Order XLI, 
Rule 33, C.P.C., is declared illegal and is hereby set aside.
