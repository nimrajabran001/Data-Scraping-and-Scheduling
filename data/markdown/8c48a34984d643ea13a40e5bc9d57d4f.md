J U D G M E N T 
 
SYED MUDASSER AMEER, J. Through this appeal filed 
under Section 9 of the Khyber Pakhtunkhwa Resolution of 
Commercial Disputes Act, 2022, the appellant has called in 
question order dated 04.12.2025 passed by learned Judge 
Consumer Protection/Resolution of Commercial Disputes, 
Haripur, whereby an application filed by the appellant under 
Section 20 of the Arbitration Act, 1940 was returned to the 
learned Civil Court on the premise that the Commercial 
Court lacked jurisdiction to entertain and decide the same. 
2. 
Briefly stated, the appellant, M/s Tariq and Saad 
Associates, entered into a Consultancy Agreement dated 
03.04.2014 with respondent M/s Coronet Foods (Pvt.) 
Limited for provision of architectural consultancy,


# Page 2

2
planning, engineering design, interior design and advisory 
services in connection with the respondent's commercial 
project situated at Haripur. Disputes subsequently arose 
between the parties regarding payment of consultancy dues. 
According to the appellant, despite rendering services in 
terms of the agreement, an amount of Rs.25,33,625/- 
remained outstanding. 
3. 
The 
Consultancy 
Agreement 
contained 
an 
arbitration clause whereby disputes arising out of or in 
connection with the agreement were to be referred to 
arbitration under the Arbitration Act, 1940. Upon failure of 
the respondent to concur in the appointment of an arbitrator, 
the appellant instituted an application under Section 20 of 
the Arbitration Act, 1940 before the learned Civil Court, 
Haripur on 21.06.2016 seeking filing of the arbitration 
agreement and reference of the dispute to arbitration. 
4. 
During pendency of the proceedings, the Khyber 
Pakhtunkhwa Resolution of Commercial Disputes Act, 
2022 (the ‘Act of 2022’) came into force. By operation of 
law, the matter stood transferred to the Commercial Court 
constituted under the said enactment. Thereafter, the learned 
Commercial Court assumed jurisdiction and actively 
supervised the arbitral process. Two arbitrators submitted 
divergent reports. An umpire was appointed on 14.09.2024. 
The umpire rendered an award on 18.02.2025. Objections 
were filed. The matter reached the stage of final 
adjudication. However, vide the impugned order dated


# Page 3

3
04.12.2025, the learned Commercial Court returned the 
proceedings to the learned Civil Court on the ground that an 
application under Section 20 of the Arbitration Act, 1940 
was required to be decided by the ordinary Civil Court and 
not by the Commercial Court. Hence, this appeal. 
5. 
Learned counsel for the appellant argued that the 
dispute admittedly arises out of a commercial consultancy 
agreement and squarely falls within the definition of a 
‘commercial dispute’ under Section 2(d) of the Act of 2022. 
It was contended that Section 3(2) confers exclusive 
jurisdiction upon Commercial Courts in respect of all suits 
and proceedings arising from or connected with commercial 
disputes. It was further argued that once the matter stood 
transferred under Section 7 of the Act of 2022 and the 
Commercial Court exercised jurisdiction throughout the 
arbitral proceedings, it could not lawfully decline 
jurisdiction at the concluding stage of the matter. 
6. 
The controversy lies within a narrow compass. The 
question is not whether the proceedings arise under the 
Arbitration Act, 1940. The real question is whether, after 
enactment of the Act of 2022, a proceeding under the 
Arbitration Act arising out of a commercial dispute falls 
within the jurisdiction of the Commercial Court or remains 
triable by the ordinary Civil Court. 
7. 
The answer must be found from a harmonious 
reading of the Arbitration Act, 1940 and the Act of 2022.


# Page 4

4
8. 
The dispute between the parties arises out of a 
consultancy agreement executed in connection with 
commercial activities and concerns a monetary claim far 
exceeding the statutory threshold prescribed under the Act 
of 2022. Section 2(d) of the Act of 2022 defines a 
‘commercial dispute’ in broad terms to include disputes 
arising out of contractual relationships relating to trade, 
business or commerce. The present controversy plainly falls 
within that definition. 
9. 
Having determined the nature of the dispute, one 
must next examine the forum designated by the legislature 
for adjudication of such disputes. Section 3(2) of the Act of 
2022 provides: 
“Subsequent to the commencement of 
this Act, all suits and proceedings, 
arising 
from 
or 
connected 
with 
commercial disputes, shall only be 
entertainable, heard and decided by the 
Commercial Courts.” 
 
10. 
The language employed by the legislature is 
significant. It does not confine itself to suits alone. It 
extends to “all suits and proceedings” arising from or 
connected with commercial disputes. Equally important are 
the words “connected with”. Those words are of the widest 
import and are intended to bring within the fold of the 
statute every proceeding having a direct nexus with the 
underlying commercial dispute.


# Page 5

5
11. 
Proceedings under Section 20 of the Arbitration 
Act do not exist in isolation. They derive their entire 
existence from the commercial dispute between the parties 
and constitute the procedural machinery selected by them 
for its resolution. A proceeding seeking filing of an 
arbitration 
agreement, 
appointment 
of 
arbitrators, 
appointment of an umpire, consideration of an award, or 
adjudication of objections thereto, is not extraneous to or 
detached from the underlying commercial dispute. It is a 
proceeding connected with and arising directly from that 
dispute. Consequently, once the underlying dispute answers 
the statutory description of a commercial dispute, 
proceedings under the Arbitration Act relating thereto 
necessarily travel along the same jurisdictional path unless 
expressly excluded by law. No such exclusion is found in 
the Act of 2022. 
12. 
The matter does not end there. The Arbitration Act 
itself points towards the same conclusion. 
13. 
Section 2(c) of the Arbitration Act defines "Court" 
as: 
“…a Civil Court having jurisdiction to 
decide the questions forming the 
subject matter of the reference if the 
same had been the subject matter of a 
suit.” 
 
14. 
The definition is functional rather than institutional. 
The statute does not ask which court originally entertained 
the proceedings. It asks which court presently possesses


# Page 6

6
jurisdiction to decide the subject matter if the dispute were 
instituted as a suit. 
15. 
If the present dispute were instituted today as a suit, 
there can be little doubt regarding the answer. By virtue of 
Sections 2(d), 3(2) and 7 of the Act of 2022, the dispute 
would be triable exclusively by the Commercial Court and 
not by the ordinary Civil Court. Therefore, for purposes of 
Section 2(c) of the Arbitration Act, the Commercial Court 
answers the statutory description of the "Court" competent 
to entertain the arbitration proceedings. 
16. 
The conclusion is further reinforced by Section 7 of 
the Act of 2022. Section 7(a) provides that all suits and 
execution petitions connected with or arising from 
commercial disputes pending in any court of original civil 
jurisdiction shall stand transferred to the concerned 
Commercial Court. Section 7(b) authorizes the Commercial 
Court to proceed with such matters from the stage at which 
they stood transferred. The legislative objective is 
continuity, not disruption. The statute contemplates a 
seamless transition from the ordinary civil forum to the 
specialized commercial forum. 
17. 
In the present case, the transfer was not effected at 
the request of either party. It occurred by operation of law. 
Upon such transfer, the Commercial Court assumed 
jurisdiction and proceeded with the matter. The arbitral 
process thereafter unfolded entirely under its supervision.


# Page 7

7
Arbitrators were appointed. Divergent reports were 
received. An umpire was appointed. An award was 
rendered. Objections were entertained. Arguments were 
heard. Having exercised jurisdiction throughout the 
proceedings and carried the matter to its final stage, the 
Commercial Court could not lawfully divest itself of 
jurisdiction by returning the matter to the ordinary Civil 
Court. 
18. 
The scheme of Section 31 of the Arbitration Act 
leads to the same destination. 
19. 
In Province of Punjab through Secretary to 
Government of Punjab v. Messrs Muhammad Tufail and 
Company (PLD 2017 SC 53), the august Supreme Court 
explained that Section 31 is designed to clothe a single 
court with jurisdiction over arbitration proceedings so as to 
avoid conflict of jurisdiction and ensure that matters once 
initiated and dealt with by one court continue before the 
same forum. 
20. 
Likewise, in Peoples Steel Mills (Pvt.) Ltd. v. Asian 
Consul Engineers (Pvt.) Ltd. (2004 MLD 1708), it was 
observed that the principal question to be kept in view is the 
jurisdiction and not the court by itself, and where 
jurisdiction stands vested in a successor forum by operation 
of law, that successor forum occupies the same position for 
purposes of the Arbitration Act.


# Page 8

8
21. 
These principles are directly attracted to the present 
case. The Commercial Court became the statutory successor 
forum upon enactment of the Act of 2022 and transfer of 
pending proceedings thereunder. To return the matter to the 
ordinary Civil Court after years of proceedings would create 
the very fragmentation and multiplicity of forums which 
Section 31 seeks to avoid. 
22. 
The impugned order also overlooks the overriding 
effect conferred by Section 10 of the Act of 2022, which 
provides that the provisions of the Act shall have effect 
notwithstanding anything inconsistent therewith contained 
in any other law for the time being in force. Thus, even if 
any doubt were assumed to exist regarding the interaction 
between the Arbitration Act and the Act of 2022, such 
doubt must yield to the express legislative command 
embodied in Section 10. 
23. 
There is yet another reason why the impugned view 
cannot be sustained. 
24. 
The Act of 2022 was enacted to establish 
specialized forums for resolution of commercial disputes. 
Its preamble recognizes the close relationship between 
commerce, economic development and expeditious dispute 
resolution. The rationale underlying specialized commercial 
courts has also received judicial recognition. In Ghulam 
Asghar Pathan v. Federation of Pakistan (PLD 2023 Sindh 
187), the Sindh High Court emphasized the necessity and


# Page 9

9
utility of specialized commercial forums for efficient and 
expeditious adjudication of commercial disputes and 
observed that commercial matters should not be submerged 
within the broader stream of ordinary civil litigation. 
25. 
Courts do not interpret statutes in a manner that 
defeats their evident purpose. The legislature created 
Commercial Courts to provide a dedicated forum for 
commercial disputes. An interpretation requiring a 
commercial dispute to leave that specialized forum after 
years of proceedings and return to the ordinary Civil Court 
would undermine the legislative design, frustrate the 
objective of specialization and delay the very adjudication 
which the statute seeks to expedite. 
26. 
The chronology of the present case itself 
demonstrates the infirmity of the impugned approach. 
Jurisdiction was not declined at the threshold. It was 
declined after transfer of the proceedings, after assumption 
of jurisdiction, after supervision of the arbitral process, after 
appointment of an umpire, after rendition of the award, and 
after hearing of objections. Such an approach neither 
advances the cause of justice nor accords with the statutory 
scheme governing commercial disputes and arbitration 
proceedings. 
27. 
For the reasons discussed above, the learned 
Commercial Court committed a material error of law in 
returning the proceedings to the learned Civil Court. The


# Page 10

10
present dispute is a commercial dispute within the meaning 
of Section 2(d) of the Act of 2022. The proceedings under 
the Arbitration Act are proceedings arising from and 
connected with that dispute. Upon transfer under Section 7, 
the Commercial Court became the competent forum within 
the contemplation of Section 2(c) of the Arbitration Act and 
was fully empowered to continue and conclude the 
proceedings. The contrary view taken in the impugned order 
cannot be sustained. 
28. 
Consequently, 
this 
appeal 
is 
allowed. 
The 
impugned order dated 04.12.2025 passed by learned Judge 
Consumer Protection/Resolution of Commercial Disputes, 
Haripur is set aside. It is declared that the learned 
Commercial Court possesses jurisdiction to entertain, 
proceed with and decide the application filed under Section 
20 of the Arbitration Act, 1940 and all proceedings arising 
therefrom or connected therewith. 
29. 
The learned Commercial Court shall resume the 
proceedings from the stage at which the matter stood 
interrupted and shall decide the same strictly in accordance 
with law through a reasoned order. 
Announced. 
21.05.2026.
