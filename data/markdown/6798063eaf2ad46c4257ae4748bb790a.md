XXXVII CPC, so that obligations evidenced 
through 
written 
acknowledgments

furnishes

liability, 
as 
finally

stands 
acknowledged in a definite quantified form

reinforced 
by 
issuance 
of 
negotiable

not constitute a denial of the principal liability 
but, at best, relate to its mode of partial 
discharge and accounting reconciliation. These 
issues fall within the domain of merits and 
adjustment and do not travel to the threshold 
question of jurisdiction under Order XXXVII 
CPC. The learned counsel for the appellant, 
with utmost respect, appears to have conflated 
the question of accounting adjustments with

liability is crystallized into a definite amount of 
Rs.7,000,000/- 
through 
written

negotiable instruments, the jurisdiction of the 
Court under Order XXXVII CPC remains fully 
attracted. The Court is not required, at this 
stage, to embark upon a roving inquiry into 
intermittent payments or alleged profit-based 
transactions, as such exercise pertains purely to 
adjustment 
on 
merits 
and 
not 
to 
the 
maintainability or competence of the Court to


# Page 13

13
proceed summarily. Order XXXVII CPC is a 
special and self-contained procedure devised 
by the legislature for expeditious enforcement 
of obligations arising out of written contracts, 
bills of exchange, hundis, promissory notes, 
and negotiable instruments. The object behind 
this special procedure is to prevent sham and 
illusory defences from delaying enforcement of 
obligations already crystallized in documentary 
form. The Indian Supreme Court in case titled 
“Mechelec Engineers & Manufacturers v. 
Basic Equipment Corporation” (1977 AIR 
577), has held that leave to defend should be 
granted only where the defence raises a fair, 
bonafide and reasonable issue. If the defence is 
illusory, sham, or practically moonshine, the 
plaintiff is entitled to summary judgment. The 
principle was revisited and elaborately restated 
in “IDBI Trusteeship Services Ltd. v. 
Hubtown Ltd.” (2017) 1 SCC 568), where the 
Supreme Court of India emphasized that the 
very purpose of summary procedure is to 
prevent delay in enforcement of commercial 
obligations supported by documents and to


# Page 14

14
ensure that defendants do not defeat clear 
claims through ingenious but unsubstantial 
defences. The ratio emerging from the above 
authorities is unmistakable: once liability 
stands acknowledged in writing and is 
supported by negotiable instruments, the Court 
is not required to reopen the entire financial 
history of the parties or undertake a forensic 
reconstruction of their business accounts. The 
inquiry remains confined to the enforceability 
of the obligation and to the existence or 
otherwise of a bonafide defence. When the 
present case is examined in the light of the 
foregoing principles, this Court finds that the 
defence raised by the appellant does not 
disclose any bonafide or substantial triable 
issue 
warranting 
interference 
with 
the 
impugned judgment. The insistence that the 
respondent was required to prove the exact 
source of funds, the dates of each individual 
advancement, or the precise financial trail by 
which the amount of Rs.7,000,000/- was 
allegedly arranged and delivered, is wholly 
misconceived in the facts and circumstances of

15
the present case. The controversy before the

reveals that after differences arose, the parties 
consciously chose to settle their disputes 
through a mutually accepted arrangement, 
where the appellant acknowledged his liability 
in a quantified amount and agreed to discharge

the realm of oral understanding alone; rather, it 
was reduced into writing through a deed

thereafter reinforced through issuance of 
negotiable instruments in favour of the 
respondent. In such circumstances, the legal 
character of the dispute underwent a significant 
transformation. The case ceased to be one of 
open, running, or unsettled accounts and 
assumed the complexion of a crystallized and 
acknowledged 
liability. 
The 
written

acknowledgment 
constituted 
a

existence and extent of his obligation, while

convert the Court into an accounting forum 
charged with reconstructing the financial 
history of parties engaged in commercial 
dealings. Such an exercise would not only be 
contrary to the spirit of summary procedure but 
would defeat the legislative object behind its 
enactment. Equally devoid of substance is the 
plea that the cheques in question were issued


# Page 17

17
merely as security instruments. The mere 
nomenclature 
attached 
to 
a 
negotiable 
instrument does not determine its legal effect. 
The law has consistently looked beyond labels 
and examined the substance of the transaction. 
Where a cheque is issued pursuant to a written 
acknowledgment of liability, the presumption 
attached to such instrument cannot be 
displaced by a bald assertion that it was 
intended merely as security. It is a settled 
principle of law that the adjudication of claims 
based on negotiable instruments is governed by 
the special rules of evidence and statutory 
presumptions provided under the Negotiable 
Instruments Act, 1881. In the present case, the 
controversy 
essentially 
hinges 
upon 
the 
statutory mandate of Section 118 of the said 
Act, which provides that “Until the contrary is 
proved, the following presumptions shall be 
made: (a) of consideration; that every 
negotiable instrument was made or drawn for 
consideration...”. This provision creates a 
mandatory legal presumption that once the 
execution of a negotiable instrument is


# Page 18

18
admitted or proved, it is deemed to have been 
issued for valid consideration. The appellant’s 
primary defense that the cheques were issued 
merely as “security” and that the respondent 
failed to prove the specific source or dates of 
the advancement of funds must be tested 
against this statutory backdrop. As held in the 
cases of Mehr Noor Muhammad v. Nazir 
Ahmed (PLD 2024 SC 45); Haji Karim And 
Another v. Zikar Abdullah (1973 SCMR 
100), Muhammad Azizur Rehman v. Liaquat 
Ali (2007 SCMR 1820); Najaf Iqbal v. 
Shahzad Rafique (2020 SCMR 1621); Malik 
Allah Ditta Malik Allah Was Aya (PKD 1956 
Lahore 521); Haider Aabdi v. Javetl Aabdi 
(1986 MLD 2298); Muhammad Ali v. Mian 
Maqbool Ahmed (2022 CLC 2115); and Syed 
Muhammad Zahir v. Mrs. Shahnaz Akhtar 
(PLD 2024 Balochistan 106), when a suit is 
based on a negotiable instrument, the initial 
burden on the plaintiff is discharged the 
moment the execution of the instrument is 
established. Thereafter, the “onus probandi” 
shifts heavily onto the defendant to rebut the


# Page 19

19
presumption under Section 118. Such rebuttal 
requires more than a mere ‘bald assertion’ or a 
‘moonshine’ defense; it necessitates the 
production of ‘independent and convincing 
evidence’ 
to 
show 
the 
absence 
of 
consideration. In the instant matter, since the 
appellant admitted to the business relationship 
and the issuance of the cheques following a 
written acknowledgment (jirga deed), the 
statutory presumption under Section 118(a) is 
fully attracted. The failure of the appellant to 
provide any evidence to the contrary means the 
presumption remains unrebutted, and the Court 
is legally bound to treat the cheques as 
instruments issued for a crystallized liability. 
Furthermore, such a plea, unsupported 
by independent and convincing evidence, 
remains a mere assertion incapable of rebutting 
the statutory presumption of liability. This 
Court 
is 
mindful 
that 
in 
commercial 
transactions parties often make periodic

however, such adjustments do not necessarily
