                            JUDGMENT 
MUHAMMAD NAEEM ANWAR, J.- The petitioner/plaintiff 
instituted a suit for declaration and perpetual injunction, asserting 
that by virtue of a No Objection Certificate (NOC) issued by 
defendants No. 1 to 3, namely the concerned municipal authorities, 
for the year 2024-2025, he had acquired an exclusive right to 
establish, manage and operate a fruit and vegetable market at Tangi 
Morh, Tehsil Tangi, District Charsadda. On the strength of the said 
NOC, the petitioner sought a declaration that defendants No. 1 to 3 
were not competent to issue any similar NOC in favour of 
defendants No. 4 to 11 or any other person for establishment of 
another fruit and vegetable market within the area. Consequential 
relief of perpetual injunction was also claimed, seeking restraint 
against defendants No. 1 to 3 from issuing any NOC to defendants 
No. 4 to 11. 
2. 
The foundation of the suit was that the petitioner had 
obtained an NOC from the municipal authorities after payment of 
the prescribed fee of Rs.20,000/-, and that issuance of such NOC 
conferred upon him an exclusive right to carry on the business of a 
fruit and vegetable market within the territorial limits of Tehsil Tangi. 
According to him, once such permission had been granted, no 
competing market could lawfully be established by any other person.

2 
 
                                              
The suit was contested by the defendants. Defendants No. 2 and 3, 
while acknowledging that the petitioner was operating an existing fruit 
and vegetable market, categorically denied that the issuance of an 
NOC conferred any monopoly or exclusive privilege upon him. 
Their stance was that the municipal authorities were legally 
competent to grant NOCs to other eligible persons in accordance 
with the applicable laws, rules and by-laws. Defendant No. 1 filed a 
separate written statement asserting that the petitioner's market was 
situated within a congested and densely populated area of the city, 
causing traffic bottlenecks, encroachments and civic inconvenience. It 
was further pleaded that there existed no legal prohibition against 
establishment of another fruit and vegetable market at a suitable 
location outside the congested urban area in order to facilitate trade, 
improve traffic management and serve the larger public interest. 
Defendants No. 4 to 11 also resisted the suit by maintaining that they 
were merely working as Arhtis (commission agents/brokers) in a fruit 
and vegetable market established outside the city limits at a distance 
of approximately two and a half kilometers from the existing market. 
All defendants sought dismissal of the suit. The learned Trial Court, 
after receipt of the written statements, proceeded to decide the 
matter summarily and, while issuing certain directions to defendants 
No. 1 to 3 to act in accordance with the relevant by-laws, restrained 
defendants No. 4 to 11 from operating fruit and vegetable markets 
both within and outside the city. Feeling aggrieved, the private 
respondents preferred an appeal which was accepted through the 
impugned judgment. Consequently, the judgment and decree of the 
learned Trial Court were set aside and the suit of the petitioner was 
dismissed, giving rise to the instant petition. Contempt of Court Case 
No. 48/1 of 2025 has also been instituted by the petitioner seeking 
initiation of contempt proceedings against the respondents for the 
alleged violation of the order passed by the learned trial Court. Since 
the said contempt petition emanates from the same civil suit which 
was decreed in favour of the petitioner by the learned trial Court, but 
the said judgment and decree have subsequently been set aside by 
the learned appellate Court through the impugned judgment, both

3 
 
                                              
matters are inextricably interconnected and arise out of the same set 
of facts and legal controversy. In order to avoid conflicting findings, 
ensure consistency in judicial determination, and secure coherence, 
rationality and judicial economy, I deem it appropriate to decide the 
Contempt of Court Case alongside the instant petition through this 
common and consolidated judgment.  
3. 
Arguments heard; Record perused. 
4. 
The record reveals that an NOC for establishment and 
operation of a fruit and vegetable market at Tangi Morh was issued 
in favour of the petitioner on 12.02.2025. A plain reading of the 
conditions attached thereto demonstrates that the permission was 
regulatory in nature and subject to municipal supervision and control. 
The NOC neither granted any proprietary interest nor conferred any 
exclusive or monopolistic right upon the petitioner. Rather, it merely 
authorized operation of the market subject to compliance with 
municipal regulations, payment of dues, observance of approved 
price lists, prevention of encroachments and renewal upon expiry of 
the prescribed period. Similarly, the record reflects that another 
NOC was issued by the Tehsil Municipal Administration, Tangi, in 
favour of Natasha Yasir for establishment of a private fruit and 
vegetable market at Chawatra Pull near Rahat Siyal House, Tangi. 
The conditions incorporated therein expressly required compliance 
with the Model By-Laws, 2021, registration of commission agents, 
maintenance of hygiene, provision of civic amenities, establishment 
of complaint redressal mechanisms, cooperation with regulatory 
authorities and observance of other municipal requirements. The 
permission was also limited in duration and revocable upon violation 
of the prescribed conditions. A comparative reading of both NOCs 
leaves no room for doubt that they are regulatory permissions 
intended to facilitate lawful commercial activity while ensuring 
compliance with municipal laws. Neither document contains any 
clause granting exclusivity or creating a monopoly in favour of its 
holder. Nor has any statutory provision, rule, regulation or by-law 
been brought to the notice of this Court which prohibits the


# Page 4

4 
 
                                              
municipal authorities from issuing more than one NOC for 
operation of fruit and vegetable markets within their territorial 
jurisdiction. 
5. 
Another significant aspect of the case is that the suit itself 
discloses no interim injunctive order operating against the municipal 
authorities at the time when the NOC was issued in favour of 
Natasha Yasir. The suit was instituted on 28.03.2025, yet the 
petitioner has failed to demonstrate that any ad-interim injunction 
had been granted restraining the authorities from processing or 
issuing permissions to other applicants. More importantly, Natasha 
Yasir, in whose favour the impugned NOC had been issued and 
whose rights stood directly affected by the relief claimed in the suit, 
was never impleaded as a party. Admittedly, defendants No. 4 to 11 
are merely Arhtis (commission agents/brokers) working in the 
market established by Natasha Yasir. This position further stands 
corroborated by the certificate issued by the Tehsil Municipal 
Administration, Tangi, dated 17.07.2025, produced during the 
course of arguments. In the absence of the principal beneficiary of 
the NOC, no effective or enforceable declaration could lawfully be 
granted. 
6. 
The entire claim of the petitioner is founded upon the 
assumption that issuance of an NOC in his favour vested him with an 
exclusive right to carry on the business of a fruit and vegetable market 
and simultaneously disabled the municipal authorities from granting 
similar permissions to others. Such contention is legally untenable. 
The right to engage in lawful trade, profession, occupation or 
business is a constitutionally protected right. The freedom to carry on 
trade and business cannot be curtailed except in accordance with law 
and for reasons sanctioned by the Constitution. Regulatory 
authorities may impose reasonable conditions in the public interest 
concerning licensing, hygiene, traffic management, environmental 
concerns, municipal planning and public safety; however, such 
regulation cannot ordinarily be transformed into a mechanism for 
creating monopolies in favour of private individuals unless expressly


# Page 5

5 
 
                                              
authorized by law. The powers vested in Tehsil Municipal 
Administrations are regulatory and supervisory in character. Their 
function is to regulate municipal affairs, maintain public order, 
ensure civic convenience, manage traffic concerns, enforce by-laws 
and facilitate lawful commercial activities within their jurisdiction. 
These powers cannot be construed as enabling the conferment of 
exclusive commercial privileges upon one trader to the exclusion of 
all others unless such exclusivity is specifically sanctioned by statute. 
No such statutory foundation has been pointed out in the present 
case. 
7. 
Equally untenable is the claim for declaration under Section 
42 of the Specific Relief Act, 1877. A declaration can only be granted 
where the plaintiff establishes the existence of a legal character or a 
right to property recognized by law and demonstrates an actual or 
threatened denial thereof by the defendant. The jurisdiction under 
Section 42 is not intended to protect commercial monopolies 
unsupported by law, nor can it be invoked to restrain lawful 
competition. The petitioner has failed to identify any legal right, 
statutory privilege or exclusive entitlement entitling him to be the sole 
operator of fruit and vegetable markets within the territorial limits of 
Tehsil Tangi. Mere issuance of an NOC does not create such a right. 
Consequently, the essential ingredients for grant of declaratory relief 
are conspicuously absent. The claim, in substance, seeks to prevent 
the municipal authorities from permitting other citizens to engage in 
a lawful business activity. Such a proposition is not only unsupported 
by law but is fundamentally inconsistent with the principles governing 
free trade and lawful competition. Healthy competition among 
traders generally promotes efficiency, availability of goods, 
competitive pricing and improved services for consumers. Courts 
cannot ordinarily grant relief that has the effect of creating a private 
monopoly at the expense of other citizens' lawful right to conduct 
business. 
8. 
The record further demonstrates that the petitioner's market 
is situated within a congested urban locality, whereas the market


# Page 6

6 
 
                                              
established by Natasha Yasir is located approximately two and a half 
kilometers away from the city. It is a matter of common experience 
that traditional fruit and vegetable markets, livestock markets, 
slaughterhouses, transport terminals and similar commercial 
establishments situated within densely populated urban centers 
frequently generate traffic congestion, sanitation issues and civic 
inconvenience. Consequently, municipal and local governments 
across the province have, over time, adopted policies aimed at 
relocating such facilities to more suitable locations outside congested 
city centers in order to ensure smooth traffic flow, better urban 
management, 
improved 
sanitation 
and 
enhanced 
public 
convenience. Such measures are rooted in public interest and fall 
squarely within the administrative and regulatory domain of local 
governments. Therefore, the decision of the municipal authorities to 
permit establishment of another market at a location outside the 
congested city area cannot, by any stretch of imagination, be termed 
arbitrary, unlawful or violative of any right of the petitioner. 
9. 
It is further noteworthy that the NOC in favour of Natasha 
Yasir had already been issued and acted upon before any effective 
restraint order was obtained by the petitioner. The law is well settled 
that injunction is essentially a preventive remedy and not one 
intended to undo acts already completed. In this regard, reference 
may be made to “Salahuddin Khan and 3 others v. Sultan-e-Rom 
and 10 others” (PLD 1973 Peshawar 95) wherein it was held that 
where the act complained of has already been completed, the grant 
of injunction becomes inappropriate. The learned appellate Court 
has correctly applied this principle while evaluating the controversy. 
10. 
The grant of NOC in favour of Natasha Yasir has not been 
shown to be contrary to any statutory provision, rule, by-law or 
regulation. Nor has any illegality, procedural irregularity or 
jurisdictional defect been pointed out in the impugned judgment. 
The findings recorded by the learned appellate Court are firmly 
grounded in the record, consistent with the governing legal principles 
and call for no interference in revisional jurisdiction.

11.  
Adverting to Contempt of Court Case (COC) No. 351-
P/2025, it is pertinent to observe that the learned trial Court decreed 
the suit in favour of the petitioner on 07.05.2025. The said judgment 
and decree, however, were subsequently reversed by the learned 
appellate Court through judgment and decree dated 19.06.2025. The 
present COC was instituted on 18.06.2025, i.e., one day prior to the 
reversal of the decree by the appellate Court. The filing of the 
contempt petition was misconceived from its very inception. So long 
as the decree of the learned trial Court remained operative, the 
petitioner had an efficacious and adequate statutory remedy under 
the Code of Civil Procedure to seek its enforcement by initiating 
execution proceedings before the competent executing Court. 
Instead of availing the legally prescribed remedy, the petitioner 
sought, through the present contempt petition, to secure execution of 
the decree indirectly under the guise of contempt proceedings, which 
is impermissible in law. Contempt jurisdiction is not intended to 
serve as a substitute for execution proceedings, nor can it be invoked 
to enforce a decree where the statute provides a complete and 
efficacious mechanism for its execution. 
12. 
Furthermore, even assuming that the petitioner alleged 
deliberate disobedience of an order passed by the learned trial 
Court, such allegation did not furnish a valid foundation for invoking 
the contempt jurisdiction of this Court. The jurisdiction to punish for 
contempt cannot be invoked merely to enforce or execute a decree 
of a subordinate Court. The proper course available to the petitioner 
was either to pursue execution in accordance with law or to seek 
such remedy as may be permissible before the Court whose order 
was allegedly violated. Consequently, the present contempt petition 
was not maintainable before this Court from its very inception. Apart 
from the above legal impediments, the very foundation of the 
contempt petition has ceased to exist. The judgment and decree of 
the learned trial Court, upon which the entire edifice of the COC 
rested, have been set aside by the learned appellate Court. Once the 
underlying decree has been reversed, no enforceable right survives


# Page 8

8 
 
                                              
thereunder, and the contempt petition, even otherwise, has become 
infructuous. 
13. 
For the reasons recorded above, the petitioner has failed to 
establish any legal or vested right warranting protection through the 
grant of declaratory or injunctive relief. The impugned judgment and 
decree passed by the learned appellate Court are free from any 
illegality, material irregularity, misreading or non-reading of evidence, 
or jurisdictional infirmity warranting interference by this Court. 
Consequently, the instant petition, being devoid of merit, is 
dismissed. Contempt of Court Case No. 351-P/2025 is also 
dismissed, being not maintainable from its inception and, in any 
event, having become infructuous upon reversal of the decree 
forming its basis. No order as to costs.
