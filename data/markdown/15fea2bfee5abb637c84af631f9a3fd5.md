  JUDGMENT 
 
MUHAMMAD NAEEM ANWAR, J.- Through the instant 
Constitutional Petition, filed under Article 199 of the Constitution of 
the Islamic Republic of Pakistan, 1973, the petitioners have called in 
question the judgment and order dated 18.10.2023 passed by the 
learned Additional District Judge-II, Charsadda, whereby the appeal 
preferred by the respondents against the order dated 14.03.2023 of the 
learned Rent Controller/Civil Judge, Charsadda, accepting the ejectment 
application and striking off the defence of the respondent, was allowed 
and the matter was remanded to the learned Rent Controller for 
decision afresh after framing of issues and recording of evidence. The 
impugned order has been assailed on the grounds of being illegal, 
arbitrary, unwarranted, without lawful authority and of no legal effect. 
2. 
I have listened to the arguments advanced by the learned counsel 
for the parties at considerable length and examined the record annexed 
with the petition.  
3. 
A careful scrutiny of the record reveals that the petitioners 
instituted an ejectment application against the respondent in respect of 
rented premises, i.e., Shop No.3106 situated near Farooq Azam Chowk, 
Mardan Road, Charsadda. The ejectment was sought on the assertions 
that the predecessor-in-interest of the petitioners, Ahbab Gul alias Gul 
Mian, was owner/landlord of the shop, which, after his demise, devolved 
upon the present petitioners through inheritance; that the premises had 
been let out to the respondent, who had been regularly paying rent, but

Rs.5,000/- per month; and further that petitioner No.3, Mian Saqib Gul,

4. 
The ejectment application was contested by the respondent 
primarily on the plea that the disputed property did not belong to the 
petitioners but was owned by the Pakhtunkhwa Highways Authority, with 
whom, according to him, he had entered into a tenancy arrangement. It 
was asserted that the shop in question had been constructed over Khasra

Department and subsequently transferred to the Pakhtunkhwa Highways 
Authority. The respondent further claimed that possession of the 
premises originally vested in his father, Sher Bahadur, and after his 
demise, he himself continued in possession thereof. 
5. 
However, the factual matrix assumes significance in view of earlier 
proceedings initiated by the respondent himself. The record demonstrates 
that prior to filing of the ejectment application on 25.11.2017, the 
respondent had instituted a civil suit on 09.11.2017, wherein petitioner 
Nos.1 and 3 were arrayed as defendants, seeking declaration and 
consequential relief on the premise that he had been in possession of 
Shop No.2 situated at Farooq Azam Chowk, Mardan Road, Charsadda, 
for a considerable period, and prior to him, his father Sher Bahadur 
remained in possession thereof. Significantly, in paragraph No.2 of the 
plaint, the respondent unequivocally pleaded that his father had been 
paying rent to the predecessor-in-interest of the present petitioners and 
that after his father, he himself continued payment of rent to them until 
notices were issued by the official authorities demanding rent in their 
favour. It was also specifically averred that the respondent intended to 
enter into a tenancy arrangement with the official authorities only after 
receipt of such notices. 
6. 
The said suit, however, did not find favour with the learned Civil 
Court and was rejected vide order dated 03.11.2018. The appeal there 
against was dismissed on 14.03.2019, while the Civil Revision Petition 
preferred before this Court also met the same fate and was dismissed on

02.10.2020. Resultantly, the respondent’s plea challenging the status of the 
petitioners stood conclusively negated. 
7. 
Simultaneously, during pendency of ejectment proceedings, the 
learned Rent Controller, taking into consideration the admitted 
relationship of landlord and tenant, vide order dated 03.11.2018, directed 
the respondent under Section 13(6) of the West Pakistan Urban Rent 
Restriction Ordinance, 1959, to deposit monthly rent at the rate of Rs. 
5,000/- before the 15th day of each month. On the same date, an 
application filed by the respondent for summary rejection of the ejectment 
petition was also dismissed. The record further reveals that the 
respondent himself expressed willingness before the learned Rent 
Controller to deposit rent at the aforesaid rate. Significantly, the order 
dated 03.11.2018 was never challenged before any competent forum and 
thus attained finality. 
8. 
Despite the unequivocal directions of the learned Rent Controller 
and notwithstanding his own undertaking, the respondent failed to comply 
with the mandatory requirement of regular deposit of rent. Consequently, 
the petitioners filed an application on 09.01.2023 for striking off the 
defence of the respondent on account of non-compliance of the order 
passed under Section 13(6) of the Ordinance of 1959. The learned Rent 
Controller, vide order dated 14.03.2023, allowed the said application and 
ordered ejectment of the respondent. However, the learned Appellate 
Court, through the impugned judgment dated 18.10.2023, set aside the 
said order and remanded the matter to the learned Rent Controller for 
framing of issues and recording of evidence, primarily on the premise that 
a preliminary issue regarding the relationship of landlord and tenant had 
not been framed. 
9. 
During the course of proceedings before this Court, the 
respondent appeared in person on the direction of the Court. Upon a 
specific query regarding possession and execution of tenancy agreement, 
he candidly admitted that his father, Sher Bahadur, had been in 
possession of the premises since long and had entered into an agreement 
with the predecessor-in-interest of the petitioners, pursuant thereto rent

was regularly paid. He further admitted that such arrangement continued 
till the year 2017 when notices were allegedly issued by the Pakhtunkhwa 
Highways Authority. He also acknowledged institution of the civil suit by 
him, dismissal thereof up to this Court, and subsequent execution of an 
agreement with the Pakhtunkhwa Highways Authority. Simultaneously, he 
conceded that proceedings initiated against him under the Khyber 
Pakhtunkhwa Public Property Removal of Encroachment Act, 1977, had 
been adjourned sine die at his own request after filing of the present 
petition. 
10. 
In the backdrop of the above factual position, the pivotal question 
requiring determination by this Court is whether the relationship of 
landlord and tenant stood established between the parties so as to justify 
the order passed by the learned Rent Controller under Section 13(6) of 
the Ordinance of 1959, and whether the learned Appellate Court was 
justified in remanding the matter for fresh determination. 
11. 
Before proceeding further, it would be advantageous to reproduce 
Section 13(6) of the West Pakistan Urban Rent Restriction Ordinance, 
1959, which reads as under: 
(6) In proceedings under this section on the first date of hearing or as soon as 
possible after that date and before issues are framed, the Controller shall direct 
the tenant to deposit all the rent due from him, and also to deposit regularly till 
the final decision of the case, before the fifteenth day of each month the 
monthly rent due from him. If there is any dispute about the amount of rent due 
or the rate of rent, the Controller shall determine such amount approximately 
and direct that same be deposited by the tenant before a date to be fixed for the 
purpose. If the tenant makes default in compliance of such an order, then if he 
is the petitioner, his application shall be dismissed summarily and if he is the 
respondent, his defence shall be struck off and the landlord put into possession 
of the property without taking further proceedings in the case. 
12. 
A plain reading of the aforesaid provision manifests that the Rent 
Controller is vested with authority to direct deposit of rent at the earliest 
stage of proceedings, even before framing of issues, where the relationship 
of landlord and tenant exists or stands admitted. It further leaves little

room for discretion once the tenant defaults in compliance, as striking off 
defence and restoration of possession to the landlord follow as statutory 
consequences. 
13. 
In the instant matter, the relationship of landlord and tenant was 
not merely inferential but expressly admitted by the respondent himself in 
his earlier civil suit, wherein in paragraph No.2 of the plaint he 
categorically acknowledged payment of rent by his predecessor as well as 
by himself to the predecessor-in-interest of the petitioners. Such 
admission constitutes substantive evidence and squarely attracts the 
doctrine of estoppel embodied in Article 115 of the Qanun-e-Shahadat 
Order, 1984. 
14. 
The contention subsequently raised by the respondent that the 
petitioners were not landlords is, therefore, wholly misconceived and self-
destructive. A litigant cannot be permitted to assume inconsistent 
positions before different fora to suit his convenience. The respondent, 
having unequivocally admitted the tenancy relationship and having acted 
upon the same by payment of rent, could not later resiled therefrom 
merely on account of notices issued by the Pakhtunkhwa Highways 
Authority. The law does not permit a party to approbate and reprobate 
simultaneously, nor can one be allowed to blow hot and cold in the same 
breath. Guidance in this regard may beneficially be sought from the 
dictum laid down in Mst. Razia Begum and another Vs. Senior Civil Judge 
(Rent Controller), Charsadda and others (PLD 1996 Peshawar 08), 
wherein it was held that where relationship of landlord and tenant stands 
admitted, the Rent Controller is competent to pass an order under Section 
13(6) of the Ordinance for deposit of rent without embarking upon a 
roving inquiry regarding title. Likewise, in Mst. Kalsoom Bibi and others 
Vs. Muhammad Amin Agha (deceased) through LRs and others (2022 
SCMR 929), the august Supreme Court reiterated that estoppel may 
ordinarily operate as a shield rather than a sword; however, where a tenant 
has himself admitted tenancy, he remains estopped from denying the title 
of the landlord during continuance of possession.

15. 
More recently, the august Supreme Court in Nawab Khan and 
another Vs. Muhammad Yousaf and others (2026 SCMR 717) 
authoritatively held that a tenant, notwithstanding any subsequent claim of 
ownership or competing title, cannot continue retaining possession as 
tenant while simultaneously disputing the landlord’s status. The proper 
recourse available to such tenant is first to surrender possession and 
thereafter agitate questions of title before a competent civil forum. 
16. 
Equally settled is the principle that compliance with an order 
passed under Section 13(6) of the Ordinance is mandatory in nature. 
Failure to deposit rent within prescribed time, even for a single day 
without legally justifiable explanation, ordinarily entails ejectment. In 
Province of Punjab and others Vs. Muhammad Jalil-ur-Rehman (1986 
SCMR 1705), it was held that the Rent Controller possesses no unfettered 
discretion to condone delay unless compelling circumstances establish 
objective impossibility. Similarly, in M.H. Mussadaq Vs. Muhammad 
Zafar Iqbal (2004 SCMR 1453), it was held that even delay of a single day, 
without lawful justification, furnishes sufficient ground for ejectment. 
Reference may also be made to Amin and others Vs. Hafiz Ghulam 
Muhammad and others (PLD 2006 SC 549) and Dr. Muhammad Bashir 
Qasim through legal heirs Vs. Gulzar Mehmood and others (2026 SCMR 
321), wherein non-compliance with statutory direction regarding deposit 
of rent was treated as a valid and sufficient basis for ejectment. 
17. 
Viewed from another angle, even if the respondent subsequently 
entered into some arrangement with the Pakhtunkhwa Highways 
Authority, such conduct did not absolve him of his legal obligations 
towards the petitioners, particularly when he admittedly entered 
possession as tenant under the predecessor-in-interest of the petitioners. 
In law, he could not alter the character of possession unilaterally. If he 
genuinely intended to attorn tenancy in favour of another authority, he was 
legally obliged to first surrender possession to the existing landlord and 
thereafter secure lawful possession under a fresh arrangement. Reliance in 
this regard may be placed upon Barkat Masih Vs. Manzoor Ahmad 
(deceased) through LRs (2006 SCMR 1068).

18. 
It is also noteworthy that the ejectment proceedings have remained 
pending since the year 2017 and the respondent, by raising mutually 
destructive pleas, has managed to prolong litigation while continuing in 
possession. Simultaneously, he sought benefit from notices issued by the 
Pakhtunkhwa Highways Authority by allegedly entering into an agreement 
with them, yet at the same time challenged those very notices before the 
Tribunal established under the Khyber Pakhtunkhwa Public Property 
Removal of Encroachment Act, 1977. Such inconsistent conduct 
unmistakably undermines the bona fides of the respondent. 
19. 
This Court is conscious of the settled principle that a Rent 
Controller is not competent to adjudicate disputed questions of title. 
However, determination of title is wholly alien to the controversy at hand. 
The only relevant inquiry pertains to existence of landlord-tenant 
relationship, which, in the present case, stands admitted from the 
respondent’s own pleadings, conduct, and statement before the Court. 
Once such relationship stood established and the respondent failed to 
comply with the mandatory direction for deposit of rent, the consequence 
envisaged under Section 13(6) of the Ordinance inexorably followed. 
20. 
Consequently, the learned Appellate Court fell in patent error 
while remanding the matter for framing of issues and recording of 
evidence regarding a fact already admitted and conclusively established on 
record. The remand order, therefore, suffers from material illegality and 
jurisdictional infirmity and cannot be allowed to sustain. 
21. 
For what has been discussed above, the instant petition is allowed. 
The impugned judgment and order dated 18.10.2023 passed by the 
learned Additional District Judge-II, Charsadda, is hereby set aside, while 
the order dated 14.03.2023 passed by the learned Rent Controller/Civil 
Judge, Charsadda, is restored. 
22. 
It is pertinent to observe that pursuant to execution proceedings 
undertaken in consequence of the restored order, possession of the 
rented premises has already been delivered to the petitioners. Therefore, 
the surviving question pertains only to the respondent’s liability regarding
