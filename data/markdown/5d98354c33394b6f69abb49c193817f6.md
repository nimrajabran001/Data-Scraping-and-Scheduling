MUHAMMAD NAEEM ANWAR, J: - The petitioner has 
questioned the legality and propriety of the judgment and 
decree dated 31.01.2025 passed by the learned Additional 
District Judge-I, Tehsil Pabbi, District Nowshera, whereby the 
appeal filed by the respondent against the judgment and order 
dated 02.03.2024 of learned Civil Judge-I, Tehsil Pabbi, 
District Nowshera, granting succession certificate, was allowed. 
Learned trial Court had granted a succession certificate by 
holding that the respondent/applicant was the sole legal heir of 
the deceased, Awalsuf Khan, and that the petitioner was not 
entitled to inherit the estate of the deceased. However, it was 
further held that the petitioner had proved the factum of debts 
to the extent of Rs. 2,33,00,000/- outstanding against the 
deceased and was, therefore, entitled to recover the said 
amount from the funds lying in the bank prior to distribution 
of the estate. The learned appellate Court, however, reversed 
the findings of the learned trial Court to the extent of the 
alleged debts.

10. 
 One of the principal submissions advanced by the 
learned counsel for the petitioner was that, in view of the 
amendments introduced through the Khyber Pakhtunkhwa 
Succession (Amendment) Act, 2019, notified on 17th 
October, 2019, the application for issuance of a succession 
certificate was not maintainable before the learned trial Court. 
According to the learned counsel, the amended statutory 
framework envisages issuance of succession certificates 
through the National Database and Registration Authority 
(NADRA) under the relevant law; therefore, the proceedings 
initiated before the learned Senior Civil Judge were 
incompetent ab initio and without lawful authority. The 
contention, however, does not merit acceptance in the peculiar 
facts and circumstances of the case. Although the amended law

5 
 
 
ordinarily provides a mechanism for issuance of succession 
certificates through the competent NADRA authorities, the 
controversy in the present matter was not confined to a simple 
or uncontested claim of succession. Rather, the determination 
of the petitioner’s status as a legal heir of the deceased involved 
disputed and intricate questions of law and fact, which could 
not conveniently be resolved through a purely administrative 
process. The statutory scheme itself recognizes that where 
complex questions relating to entitlement, heirship, or 
competing claims arise, the matter may legitimately fall within 
the jurisdiction of the Court exercising powers in succession 
proceedings. In such circumstances, the learned Court was 
competent to undertake a prima facie inquiry for purposes of 
determining entitlement to succession in exercise of its 
summary jurisdiction. The finding recorded by the learned 
Senior Civil Judge with respect to the petitioner’s entitlement 
as a legal heir, having been rendered within the ambit of 
succession proceedings and upon appreciation of the available 
material, does not call for interference in constitutional 
jurisdiction, particularly when no jurisdictional defect or patent 
illegality has been demonstrated. Therefore, the mere 
existence of an administrative mechanism through NADRA 
for issuance of succession certificates does not, by itself, render 
the proceedings before the learned Court incompetent where 
adjudication of disputed heirship or complex questions of 
entitlement becomes necessary.

Insofar as the second question is concerned, it is an 
admitted position that Awalsuf Khan was not survived by any 
sharer. Consequently, succession devolves upon the residuary 
(Asabah) according to the recognized order of succession. 
Under Sunni Hanafi law, a full brother ranks at Serial No. 5 in 
the table of residuary, whereas a full brother's son ranks at

6 
 
 
Serial No. 9. It is a settled principle of Muhammadan law that, 
among residuary, the nearer in degree excludes the more 
remote. Therefore, the full brother, being the nearer 
residuary, excludes the full brother's son from inheritance. In 
the present case, the respondent, being the full brother of the 
deceased, rightly excluded the petitioner, who claims as the 
son of a predeceased full brother. The rule of exclusion 
applies (i.e., the nearer in degree excludes the more 
remote.) Reliance in this regard is placed upon “Lal and others 
vs. Barkhurdar and others” (1996 SCMR 1964) and “Kabir 
Hussain v. Mst. Iqbal Bibi” (1991 MLD 1681), “Waris Ali and 
others vs. Rasoolan Bibi” (PLD 2014 SC 779), “Saadullah and 
others vs. Mst. Gulbanda and others” (2014 SCMR 1205).  
12.  
Moreover, Section 372 of the Succession Act, 1925 
governs the issuance of a succession certificate, which is 
confined only to “debts” and “securities” of a deceased person, 
as contemplated under the provisions of the Act ibid. A 
succession certificate neither confers any general authority for 
administration of the estate of the deceased nor constitutes a 
declaration of title or final determination of heirship in favour 
of the grantee. Its scope is limited and summary in nature, 
intended merely to authorize the holder to realize debts and 
collect securities due to the deceased, while simultaneously 
affording indemnity and protection to debtors making 
payment in good faith to the certificate-holder. The underlying 
object of issuance of a succession certificate is, therefore, to 
facilitate prompt realization of debts and securities of the 
deceased, to regulate succession in a limited fiscal sense, to 
avoid multiplicity of disputes regarding entitlement, and to 
protect persons dealing bona fide with the ostensible legal 
representative from future claims or liability. It is not a


# Page 7

7 
 
 
document of title, nor does it conclusively determine 
proprietary or inheritance rights inter se the legal heirs.  
13. 
In addition, the Succession Act, 1925 provides a special 
and summary procedure for adjudication of applications 
seeking issuance of a succession certificate in respect of the 
debts and securities of a deceased person. While Section 372 
regulates the institution of such proceedings, Section 381 of 
the Act, 1925 delineates the legal effect of a certificate so 
issued. The underlying object of the statutory scheme is to 
facilitate expeditious realization of debts and securities 
belonging to the estate of the deceased without embroiling the 
parties in protracted disputes concerning title or succession. A 
succession certificate, however, neither adjudicates the 
proprietary rights of the persons claiming interest in the estate 
nor conclusively determines their status as legal heirs or their 
respective shares in the recoverable assets. Its issuance is 
summary in character and confined to authorizing the 
certificate-holder to collect debts and realize securities due to 
the deceased. The legislative rationale behind such certificate 
is primarily to indemnify and protect the debtor or obligor 
making payment in good faith to the holder thereof, so that 
such payment may operate as a valid discharge against future 
claims. Nonetheless, the holder of the certificate does not 
acquire absolute ownership or exclusive title over the amount 
so recovered; rather, he remains under a legal obligation to 
account for and distribute the realized amount amongst all 
persons lawfully entitled thereto in accordance with their 
respective legal shares and rights. Thus, a certificate issued 
under the Succession Act, 1925 does not confer title, 
ownership, or final declaration of succession upon the grantee, 
but merely furnishes an enabling authority to recover debts 
and securities on behalf of the estate of the deceased.

14. 
In the same scenario, irrespective of the remand order 
of this court, it is a settled principle of law that any debt, 
liability, or lawful claim alleged to be recoverable from a 
deceased person does not automatically become enforceable 
against his legal heirs merely by reason of succession. Such 
liability can only be enforced against the legal representatives 
to the extent of the estate inherited by them and subject to a 
lawful adjudication by a competent Court of law. Before 
fastening liability upon the legal heirs, the existence, nature, 
and quantum of the claim must first be judicially determined 
through appropriate proceedings, wherein the alleged debt is 
established in accordance with law. A Court exercising 
jurisdiction in succession proceedings under the Succession 
Act, 1925 is not vested with authority to adjudicate disputed 
claims of debt, determine contested liabilities, or secure 
alleged recoveries in favour of an objector whose claim 
remains unsubstantiated and has not been crystallized through 
an independent suit or competent adjudication. The 
jurisdiction of the Succession Court is summary and limited in 
nature, confined merely to determining entitlement for the 
issuance of a succession certificate in respect of debts and 
securities of the deceased, and does not extend to deciding 
complicated questions relating to liability or recovery. In the 
instant matter, the objector had admittedly instituted a suit for 
recovery of the alleged amount along with other prayers before 
the competent court however, for the reasons best known to 
him it was dismissed as withdrawn. Therefore, the learned trial 
Court has transgressed the limits of its statutory jurisdiction by 
granting a decree for his alleged debts. Though the appeal 
Court has allowed the appeal but not on account of jurisdiction 
which order too to that extent was not correct. Such exercise 
of authority being dehors the law and beyond the scope of 
succession proceedings was without lawful jurisdiction and,

Equally misconceived is the contention that jurisdiction stood 
vested in the Court on account of consent of the parties. It is 
by now a trite principle of law that consent, acquiescence, 
waiver, or concession of parties can neither create nor enlarge 
the jurisdiction of a Court where none exists under the law. 
Jurisdiction flows from statute and must be exercised strictly 
within the bounds prescribed by the legislature; therefore, a 
defect of inherent jurisdiction cannot be cured merely by 
agreement or consent of litigants. Consequently, any 
assumption of jurisdiction by the learned Court below for 
adjudication of disputed liabilities in succession proceedings, 
merely on the basis of consent of the parties, was patently 
without lawful authority and unsustainable in law.  
16. 
Furthermore, a Court seized of an application for 
issuance of a succession certificate is obligated to follow the 
procedure prescribed under Section 373 of the Succession 
Act, 1925, which is expressly summary in character and limited 
in scope. Proceedings under the said provision are not 
intended to finally adjudicate disputed rights of succession, 
determine title inter se the claimants, or conclusively establish 
entitlement to the debts and securities of the deceased. Rather, 
the legislative object is confined to facilitating collection of 
debts and securities through a prima facie determination so as 
to avoid undue hardship in administration of the estate. The 
statutory framework itself recognizes the limitations of 
succession proceedings. Section 373(3) of the Act, 1925 
unequivocally 
provides 
that 
where 
determination 
of 
entitlement necessitates adjudication of intricate and 
complicated questions of law or fact, which cannot 
conveniently be resolved in summary proceedings, the Court 
is not required to conclusively determine such controversies. 
Instead, it may grant the certificate to the person who appears,


# Page 11

11 
 
 
on a prima facie assessment, to possess the best title thereto. 
Thus, the standard applicable in succession proceedings is not 
proof beyond dispute, but merely a tentative satisfaction 
regarding preferential entitlement for purposes of collection. 
Similarly, Section 373(4) contemplates a situation where 
multiple applicants claim interest in the estate of the deceased. 
In such circumstances, the Court may consider the nature and 
extent of their apparent interest, along with their fitness and 
suitability, while deciding to whom the certificate ought to be 
issued. Even then, such determination remains provisional and 
administrative in nature, rather than a final adjudication of 
proprietary rights. It is, therefore, a settled proposition of law 
that determination of title, declaration of inheritance rights, 
adjudication of disputed shares, or resolution of complex 
questions concerning entitlement fall beyond the jurisdictional 
scope of succession proceedings. A succession certificate 
merely authorizes the holder to recover debts and securities of 
the deceased on a prima facie basis and does not extinguish or 
determine rival claims. Any person seeking a definitive 
adjudication regarding title, entitlement, or extent of shares 
must seek recourse through a substantive suit before a Court 
of competent jurisdiction, wherein disputed questions of law 
and fact may be properly tried and conclusively resolved.

alleged debts and liabilities of the deceased falls outside the 
limited and summary jurisdiction of a Court seized of

succession proceedings under the Succession Act, 1925, 
whose mandate is confined to a prima facie determination for 
issuance of a succession certificate and not adjudication of 
disputed claims requiring detailed evidence. Such contested 
questions could only have been adjudicated through a 
substantive suit before a Court of competent jurisdiction. The 
record further reveals that the petitioner had, in fact, availed 
the appropriate legal remedy by instituting a suit for 
determination and recovery of the alleged claim; however, the 
same was subsequently withdrawn by him of his own accord. 
Having voluntarily abandoned the proper forum for 
adjudication, the petitioner cannot now seek revival or 
determination of the very same disputed claim through 
succession proceedings, nor can the matter be remanded for 
such purpose, particularly when the legal impediment has 
arisen due to the petitioner’s own conduct. A litigant cannot be 
permitted to create a procedural vacuum by withdrawing the 
competent proceedings and thereafter seek to circumvent the 
statutory limitations governing succession jurisdiction. 
18. 
 The learned appellate Court has rightly appreciated the 
legal and factual controversies involved in the matter, which 
justifiably culminated in the acceptance of the appeal. The 
findings recorded by the learned appellate Court are founded 
upon a proper appraisal of the material available on record 
and a correct understanding of the limited scope and 
jurisdiction governing succession proceedings under the law. 
Learned counsel for the petitioner has remained unable to 
point out any illegality, material irregularity, jurisdictional 
defect, misreading, or non-reading of evidence in the 
impugned judgment, particularly with regard to the grant of 
succession certificate in favour of the entitled party. It is by now 
a settled proposition of law that a Court exercising jurisdiction


# Page 13

13 
 
 
in succession proceedings is not competent to adjudicate 
disputed liabilities of the deceased, determine contested debts, 
or pass orders fastening financial obligations upon the estate, 
as such matters fall beyond the summary scope contemplated 
under the Succession Act, 1925, and are required to be 
determined through independent substantive proceedings 
before a Court of competent jurisdiction. Since no lawful order 
regarding the alleged liability of the deceased for payment of 
disputed amounts could have been passed by the Court 
granting the succession certificate, the interference made by 
the learned appellate Court to that extent was fully justified and 
in consonance with the settled principles of law. The 
impugned judgment, being well-reasoned, legally sound, and 
based upon proper appreciation of the evidence and 
applicable legal framework, warrants no interference in the 
exercise of revisional jurisdiction, which is otherwise limited in 
scope and cannot be invoked merely to substitute one 
plausible view with another in the absence of patent illegality 
or jurisdictional error. The revision petition in hand is thus 
dismissed. No order as to costs.
