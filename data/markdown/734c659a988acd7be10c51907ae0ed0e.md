Having given anxious consideration to the

this Court is of the considered view that the controversy 
involved in the present and connected petitions does not

Urban Immovable Property Tax Act, 1958. Likewise, the 
determination of these petitions does not hinge upon 
whether the properties owned by the petitioners are 
situated in urban or rural localities. The principal question 
requiring adjudication is whether the mandatory statutory 
requirements envisaged under Sections 42 and 44 of the 
Act of 2013 were duly complied with before the levy, 
assessment and recovery of Urban Immovable Property

necessarily depends upon the scope, effect and interplay of

examine the statutory scheme embodied in Section 44 of

of reference;

(2) The rate of property tax in an area within a tehsil shall

Provided that in the areas within a tehsil where rate has not

17. 
 
A plain reading of Section 44 demonstrates 
that the Legislature has enacted a comprehensive statutory 
framework governing the levy, determination and recovery 
of Urban Immovable Property Tax. Under sub-section (1), 
every tehsil is declared to be a rating area within the 
meaning of the Urban Immovable Property Tax Act, 1958. 
Sub-section (2) further mandates that the rate of property 
tax applicable within any area of a tehsil shall be subject to 
the approval of the respective Tehsil Council. The proviso 
appended thereto assumes particular significance, as it 
expressly provides that where no rate has been determined, 
the area concerned shall be deemed exempt from property 
tax until such determination is made. Sub-section (3), in 
turn, preserves continuity by stipulating that the existing 
rates shall remain in force unless varied in accordance with 
sub-section (2). When construed as a whole, Section 44 
delineates not only the territorial application of the tax but 
also the statutory mechanism through which the applicable 
rates are to be lawfully determined, approved and enforced. 
18. 
 
Learned counsel appearing on behalf of the 
respondents contended that, by virtue of sub-section (3) of 
Section 44, the rates prevailing under the previous local 
government regime continued automatically and remained 
enforceable, with the result that no fresh determination or 
approval by the respective Tehsil Council was required. 
Although the submission appears attractive at first glance, 
it does not withstand close scrutiny. Acceptance of such an


# Page 11

11
interpretation would substantially undermine, if not 
altogether render redundant, both sub-section (2) and the 
proviso appended thereto. It is a settled principle of 
statutory interpretation that every provision enacted by the 
Legislature must, as far as possible, be assigned meaning 
and effect, and that no part of a statute should be construed 
in a manner that renders it superfluous, nugatory or devoid 
of practical significance. A construction which nullifies the 
express requirement of approval by the Tehsil Council or 
renders the deeming exemption contained in the proviso 
ineffective cannot, therefore, be regarded as a permissible 
interpretation of Section 44. 
19. 
 
Section 44 must be construed as a cohesive 
and integrated statutory scheme, with each of its 
components being assigned its due meaning and effect. 
Sub-section (2) unequivocally designates the Tehsil 
Council as the competent authority for the determination 
and approval of the applicable rate of property tax. The 
proviso appended thereto further prescribes the legal 
consequence 
flowing 
from 
the 
absence 
of 
such 
determination, namely, that the area concerned shall be 
deemed exempt from property tax until the rate is lawfully 
determined. Sub-section (3), when read in harmony with 
the preceding provisions, merely preserves continuity in 
respect of rates already lawfully approved, determined or 
operative under the existing legal framework and obviates 
the necessity of repeated legislative or administrative


# Page 12

12
action. It cannot be construed in isolation or interpreted in 
a manner that defeats the legislative mandate embodied in 
sub-section (2), renders the role of the Tehsil Council 
redundant, or nullifies the statutory exemption expressly 
created by the proviso. Such a construction alone gives 
meaningful effect to every part of Section 44 and preserves 
the internal coherence of the statutory scheme enacted by 
the Legislature. 
20. 
 
Particular 
significance 
attaches 
to 
the 
expression “shall be deemed to be exempt” employed by 
the Legislature in the proviso to Section 44(2). It is a 
settled principle of law that where the Legislature creates a 
legal fiction, the Court is under a duty to give full effect to 
the fiction so created and to carry it to its logical and 
inevitable conclusion. Once the Legislature has expressly 
declared that an area shall be deemed exempt from 
property tax until the applicable rate is determined, such 
declaration cannot be ignored, diluted or rendered 
ineffective through a process of interpretation. The 
statutory exemption embodied in the proviso continues to 
operate with full force unless and until the rate is lawfully 
determined and approved by the authority designated under 
the statute. The necessary consequence, therefore, is that in 
the absence of a valid determination or approval of rates by 
the competent authority, no enforceable liability to Urban 
Immovable Property Tax can arise, nor can any

13
assessment, demand or recovery proceedings be sustained 
in law.

Before proceeding further, it would be 
advantageous to address another important aspect of the 
controversy which surfaced during the course of 
arguments, namely, the nature, scope and legal effect of 
the powers conferred upon local governments under the 
constitutional and statutory framework governing local 
taxation. Since a substantial part of the respondents’ case 
rests upon the authority purportedly exercised by 
administrative functionaries in matters relating to the 
determination and approval of property tax rates, it 
becomes necessary to examine whether such powers are 
administrative in character or whether they partake of a 
legislative or quasi-legislative nature under the scheme of 
the Constitution and the Khyber Pakhtunkhwa Local 
Government Act, 2013. 
22. 
 
Learned counsel appearing on behalf of the 
respondents contended that Article 140-A of the 
Constitution merely envisages the devolution of political, 
administrative and financial responsibility to local 
governments and does not confer upon them any 
legislative authority in the constitutional sense. Proceeding 
on this premise, it was argued that the determination or 
approval of rates under Sections 42 and 44 of the Khyber 
Pakhtunkhwa Local Government Act, 2013 constitutes a 
purely administrative function and, therefore, does not


# Page 14

14
require exercise by an elected local council. According to 
the respondents, the powers relating to approval or 
determination of property tax rates could validly be 
exercised by administrative functionaries acting under the 
statutory framework governing local governments. The 
submission, therefore, necessitates an examination of the 
nature and character of the powers entrusted under 
Sections 42 and 44 of the Act of 2013 and the 
constitutional status of local governments in matters 
pertaining to taxation. 
23. 
 
We are unable to subscribe to such a broad 
proposition. 
It 
is 
undoubtedly 
correct 
that 
local 
governments do not enjoy plenary legislative competence 
comparable to that vested in Parliament or the Provincial 
Assemblies. However, it does not follow that they are 
entirely devoid of legislative or quasi-legislative authority. 
The distinction between primary legislation and delegated 
legislation is now firmly embedded in constitutional and 
administrative jurisprudence. While local governments 
cannot enact laws in the constitutional sense, they may 
validly exercise delegated, subordinate or quasi-legislative 
powers where such authority has expressly been conferred 
upon them by the Legislature. Indeed, where a statute 
entrusts a local government institution with the authority to 
determine rates, impose taxes, frame bye-laws or regulate 
matters of general application within its jurisdiction, the 
exercise of such authority transcends the realm of purely


# Page 15

15
administrative action and assumes a legislative or quasi-
legislative character. The correctness of the respondents’ 
contention must, therefore, be examined in the light of 
these well-settled principles. 
24. 
 
In Rizwan Zaka Gill versus Government of 
Punjab (PLD 2015 Lahore 28), the Lahore High Court 
observed that local governments do not possess legislative 
powers in the same manner as federal or provincial 
legislatures. Those observations, however, were rendered 
in the context of the constitutional status, composition and 
electoral structure of local government institutions and 
were not intended to negate every form of delegated or 
subordinate legislative authority conferred upon such 
bodies by statute. The judgment, therefore, cannot be read 
as laying down an absolute proposition that local 
governments are incapable of exercising powers of a 
legislative or quasi-legislative nature where the Legislature 
has expressly entrusted them with such functions. Properly 
understood, the decision merely recognizes the distinction 
between plenary legislative competence, which vests in 
Parliament and the Provincial Assemblies, and delegated 
legislative authority, which may validly be exercised by 
local government institutions within the limits prescribed 
by law. 
25.  
 
Conversely, in All Pakistan Textile Mills 
Association versus Province of Sindh (2004 YLR 192), 
the Sindh High Court recognized that although taxation is


# Page 16

16
essentially a legislative function, the Legislature may 
authorize local councils to impose taxes subject to 
conditions prescribed by law. Likewise, the august 
Supreme Court of Pakistan in Province of Punjab versus 
Market Committee, Okara (2011 SCMR 1856) identified 
the power to frame bye-laws, impose taxes and administer 
local funds as essential attributes of a local authority. Such 
powers, though not legislative in the plenary constitutional 
sense, nevertheless possess a delegated or quasi-legislative 
character. 
26. 
 
The legal position emerging from the 
foregoing authorities is that, although local governments 
do not possess independent legislative competence, the 
exercise of powers relating to the determination of taxes, 
rates, fees or other matters of general application assumes 
a delegated or quasi-legislative character where such 
authority has been entrusted by the Legislature. The 
determination of rates under Section 44(2) of the Act of 
2013 squarely falls within this category, as it directly 
affects the proprietary and fiscal rights of persons situated 
within the relevant territorial jurisdiction. 
27. 
 
It is, therefore, not possible to characterize 
the function entrusted under Section 44(2) as a purely 
administrative act. The determination or approval of rates 
is a normative exercise affecting an entire class of 
taxpayers and, therefore, possesses a legislative or, at the 
very least, a quasi-legislative character. Such authority


# Page 17

17
must, unless the statute expressly provides otherwise, be 
exercised by the institution designated by the Legislature 
itself. 
28. 
 
Support for this interpretation may also be 
drawn from the recent judgment of the Lahore High Court 
in Mushtaq Ahmad and others versus Government of 
Punjab etc. (2025 LHC 7814), wherein pari materia 
provisions relating to rating areas and property taxation 
were examined. The Lahore High Court held that 
legislative declarations contained in local government 
enactments were sufficient to constitute rating areas, while 
continuity clauses were intended to preserve taxation in 
areas already subjected to property tax under the previous 
statutory regime. 
29. 
 
The significance of the aforesaid judgment 
lies in its recognition that legislative declarations relating 
to rating areas and provisions governing the determination 
of rates perform distinct functions within the statutory 
scheme. The declaration of a territory as a rating area does 
not, by itself, determine the applicability, extent or 
enforceability of tax liability. The provisions relating to the 
determination of 
rates 
must, 
therefore, 
be 
given 
independent and meaningful effect. 
30. 
 
In the present case, the controversy does not 
revolve around whether the petitioners’ localities form part 
of a rating area. That issue stands settled by Section 44(1) 
of the Act of 2013 as well as by the judgment rendered by


# Page 18

18
this Court in Askari Cement Limited. The real question 
requiring determination is whether the mandatory 
requirements embodied in Sections 42(2) and 44(2) of the 
Act were complied with before the impugned assessments 
and recovery proceedings were initiated. 
31. 
 
The proviso appended to Section 44(2) 
assumes decisive importance in this regard. The 
Legislature has expressly provided that where no rate has 
been determined, the area concerned shall be deemed 
exempt from property tax until such determination is made. 
It is a settled principle that a legal fiction created by statute 
must be given its full and logical effect. Consequently, 
unless rates are lawfully determined by the competent 
authority designated by the Legislature, the statutory 
exemption continues to operate and no enforceable liability 
to property tax can arise. 
32. 
 
The 
interpretation 
advanced 
by 
the 
respondents would effectively render both the proviso to 
Section 44(2) and the requirement of approval by the 
Tehsil Council redundant. If historical rates were 
automatically enforceable throughout every locality within 
a tehsil irrespective of whether the competent council had 
ever exercised its statutory authority, there would have 
been no necessity for the Legislature to incorporate the 
deeming exemption contained in the proviso. Such a 
construction would offend the settled principle of statutory 
interpretation that every provision enacted by the


# Page 19

19
Legislature must be assigned meaning and effect, and that 
no part of a statute should be treated as superfluous, 
redundant or without purpose. 
33. 
 
The only interpretation that harmonizes all 
components of Section 44 is that sub-section (1) declares 
every tehsil to be a rating area; sub-section (2) entrusts the 
determination of rates to the competent Tehsil Council; the 
proviso grants statutory exemption where no rate has been 
determined; and sub-section (3) preserves continuity only 
in respect of rates already lawfully operative in areas 
previously subjected to Urban Immovable Property Tax. 
Each provision thus operates within its own sphere without 
nullifying or rendering redundant the others. 
34. 
 
The above interpretation receives further 
support from Section 42(2) of the Khyber Pakhtunkhwa 
Local Government Act, 2013, which expressly provides 
that no tax shall be levied without prior publication of the 
proposal, invitation and consideration of objections, and 
approval 
of 
the 
respective 
local 
council. 
These 
requirements are not mere procedural formalities; rather, 
they constitute substantive safeguards incorporated by the 
Legislature to ensure transparency, accountability and 
public participation in matters of local taxation. The 
statutory scheme, therefore, entrusts taxation decisions to 
representative local institutions and not to executive 
functionaries acting in their administrative capacity.

35. 
 
It may further be observed that the functions 
assigned to Tehsil Councils under the Act of 2013 include 
the approval of taxes, fines and penalties. Such powers are 
legislative and financial in nature. Taxation directly affects 
the proprietary rights and financial obligations of citizens 
and, 
therefore, 
cannot 
be 
equated 
with 
routine 
administrative functions. The requirement of approval by 
an elected local council reflects the legislative intent that 
taxation decisions should emanate from representative 
institutions 
possessing 
democratic

and 
accountability.

The aforesaid contention, in our considered 
view, is devoid of substance. Section 120-A was 
introduced as a transitional provision following the 
dissolution of local governments and was intended to 
ensure continuity in the administration of local affairs 
during the interregnum. Even after its subsequent

38. 
 
The 
distinction 
between 
administrative 
powers, on the one hand, and legislative and financial 
powers, on the other, is fundamental and cannot be 
overlooked. Article 140-A of the Constitution envisages a


# Page 22

22
democratic local government system vested with political, 
administrative and financial responsibility. The power to 
levy, approve or determine taxes is essentially legislative 
and financial in character and has been expressly entrusted 
by statute to elected local councils. Such power directly 
affects the proprietary and fiscal rights of citizens and, 
therefore, cannot be presumed to have been delegated or 
transferred to executive officers in the absence of clear and 
unequivocal legislative authorization. The law laid down 
by this Court in Muhammad Tahir and others v. 
Government of Khyber Pakhtunkhwa and others (PLD
