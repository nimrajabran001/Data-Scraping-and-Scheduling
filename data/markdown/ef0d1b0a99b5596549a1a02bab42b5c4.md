2. 
Brief 
facts

that 
the 
respondent/plaintiff instituted a suit under Order 
XXXVII, C.P.C. against one Umair Gul for recovery of

Rs.14,00,000/- along with profit. The said suit was 
contested by the defendant but ultimately decreed in 
favour of the respondent/plaintiff. 
3. 
The respondent/decree-holder thereafter initiated 
execution proceedings before the learned Additional 
District Judge-VII, Abbottabad. During the course of 
execution proceedings, the present appellants furnished 
surety bonds before the learned Executing Court, 
undertaking that in case the judgment-debtor failed to 
appear before the Court, they would produce him and 
that if he failed to satisfy the decretal amount, they 
themselves would be responsible for payment thereof. 
4. 
Subsequently, 
the 
appellants 
moved 
an 
application seeking withdrawal of the surety bonds. The 
learned Executing Court, vide impugned order dated 
02.11.2023, dismissed the said application. Hence, the 
instant appeal. 
5. 
Arguments heard. Record perused. 
6. 
It is an admitted position that the judgment and 
decree passed in favour of the respondent/decree-holder 
have attained finality. The controversy regarding the 
rights and liabilities of the parties already stands 
conclusively determined. The execution proceedings are 
now confined to enforcement of the decree and securing 
to the decree-holder the fruits thereof.

8. 
The language employed in the surety bond 
leaves no room for ambiguity. The appellants not only 
undertook to secure the presence of the judgment-debtor 
but also expressly bound themselves to satisfy the 
decretal amount in the event of default by him. 
9. 
Such an undertaking is not a mere formality. It is 
a solemn assurance furnished before a Court of law. 
Once accepted and acted upon by the Court, it creates 
legal consequences which cannot be ignored merely 
because the surety subsequently changes his mind. 
10. 
Learned counsel for the appellants argued that 
the appellants are entitled to revoke or withdraw the 
surety bonds and, in support of such contention, placed 
reliance upon section 130 of the Contract Act, 1872. 
11. 
The argument is misconceived.


# Page 4

4
12. 
Chapter VIII of the Contract Act, 1872 deals 
with contracts of guarantee. Section 128 expressly 
provides that the liability of the surety is co-extensive 
with that of the principal debtor unless otherwise 
provided by the contract. The legal consequence is that 
the surety and the principal debtor become jointly and 
severally liable for the obligation undertaken. The 
creditor is not bound to first exhaust his remedies against 
the principal debtor before proceeding against the surety. 
This principle has consistently been affirmed by the 
superior Courts. Reference may be made to Mst. Zubaida 
Begum v. Mst. Marium Bibi (2006 SCMR 619), 
Muhammad Ashraf v. Habib Bank Limited (2005 SCMR 
72), Muhammad Shahzad v. Muhammad Arshad (2023 
CLC 2169) and Muhammad Riaz v. Muhammad Aslam 
(2025 CLC 1820). 
13. 
Thus, once the appellants voluntarily undertook 
before the learned Executing Court that they would 
satisfy the decretal amount in the event of default by the 
judgment-debtor, their liability became co-extensive with 
that of the judgment-debtor himself. The decree-holder 
was not legally required to first exhaust every possible 
remedy against the judgment-debtor before seeking 
enforcement of the surety bond. 
14. 
The Contract Act draws a clear distinction 
between an ordinary guarantee and a continuing 
guarantee. Section 129 defines a continuing guarantee as


# Page 5

5
one extending to a series of transactions. Section 130 
permits revocation only of such continuing guarantee in 
respect of future transactions. 
15. 
The undertaking furnished by the appellants is 
not a continuing guarantee within the contemplation of 
Sections 129 and 130 of the Contract Act. It does not 
relate to a series of future transactions. It was furnished 
in relation to a specific and existing liability arising from 
a decree already passed by a competent Court. The 
liability was fixed, quantified and judicially determined. 
Consequently, section 130 of the Contract Act has no 
application to the facts of the present case. 
16. 
Equally significant is the fact that the Contract 
Act itself specifies the circumstances in which a surety 
may stand discharged. Sections 133 to 139 deal with 
discharge by variance of contract, release of principal 
debtor, composition, impairment of remedy and other 
recognized grounds. No provision confers upon a surety 
an unrestricted right to unilaterally withdraw from a 
guarantee after it has been accepted and acted upon. 
17. 
The legislative scheme therefore leaves little 
room for doubt. A surety may be discharged in 
circumstances recognized by law or by contract, but not 
merely because he has changed his mind or subsequently 
finds the obligation inconvenient.

6
18. 
The position under the Code of Civil Procedure 
is equally significant. Section 145, C.P.C. expressly 
recognizes and enforces liabilities undertaken by sureties 
before Courts. The provision is founded upon the 
principle that a person who voluntarily undertakes 
responsibility before a Court becomes answerable in the 
same proceedings without compelling the successful 
litigant to institute fresh litigation. 
19. 
Significantly, neither Section 145, C.P.C. nor 
any other provision of the Code confers a right upon a 
surety, who has undertaken responsibility for satisfaction 
of a decree, to unilaterally withdraw from such 
undertaking after it has been accepted by the Court. 
20. 
It is true that Order XXXVIII Rule 3, C.P.C. 
contains a provision regarding discharge of surety 
furnished for appearance. However, that provision 
pertains to an entirely different situation. The very 
existence of such an express provision demonstrates that 
whenever the legislature intended to permit discharge of 
a surety, it did so expressly. No analogous provision 
exists 
in 
respect 
of 
a 
surety 
who 
undertakes 
responsibility for payment of decretal amount. The 
omission is both deliberate and significant. 
21. 
The Courts have consistently held that a surety 
who voluntarily furnishes an undertaking before a Court 
cannot subsequently wriggle out of the obligation


# Page 7

7
assumed by him. In Muhammad Shahzad v. Muhammad 
Arshad (2023 CLC 2169) and Muhammad Riaz v. 
Muhammad Aslam (2025 CLC 1820), it was held that 
where a person voluntarily appears before the Court, 
furnishes surety and undertakes responsibility for 
satisfaction of a decree, he cannot subsequently resile 
from such undertaking after the Court and the decree-
holder have acted upon it. 
22. 
The rationale is obvious. If sureties are permitted 
to withdraw at will after securing the benefit for which 
the surety was furnished, the entire object of obtaining 
sureties in judicial proceedings would stand frustrated. 
Such a course would undermine confidence in judicial 
undertakings and seriously prejudice decree-holders who 
act upon such assurances. 
23. 
A surety who undertakes before a Court to 
satisfy a decree in the event of default by the judgment-
debtor does not possess an independent statutory right to 
withdraw from that undertaking merely at his own 
election. Such liability continues unless discharged in 
accordance with law or the terms of the contract itself. 
24. 
The authorities further establish that subsequent 
events do not ordinarily discharge a surety once liability 
has attached. Attempts by sureties to avoid liability on 
account of arrest, imprisonment or even death of the


# Page 8

8
judgment-debtor have repeatedly been rejected by the 
Courts. 
25. 
In Muhammad Riaz v. Muhammad Aslam (2025 
MLD 1156), it was specifically held that even the death 
of the principal obligor does not by itself discharge the 
surety unless the bond itself so provides. The broader 
principle emerging from the jurisprudence is that once 
liability has attached under a valid surety bond, it 
survives unless law or contract provides otherwise. No 
such provision has been pointed out in the present case. 
26. 
Guidance may also be drawn from Muhammad 
Amin v. Judge Family Court, Sahiwal and others (2015 
YLR 316) and Javed Iqbal v. Judge Family Court and 
others (2018 MLD 1296), wherein it was held that a 
surety is as much bound by his undertaking as the 
judgment-debtor himself and both remain collectively 
and severally liable for satisfaction of the decree. The 
surety cannot evade the obligation voluntarily assumed 
before the Court. 
27. 
The sanctity of undertakings furnished before 
Courts cannot be diluted. Judicial proceedings rest upon 
confidence that assurances given to the Court will be 
honoured. A person who voluntarily assumes liability 
before a Court cannot ordinarily be permitted to retreat 
from such obligation once the Court and the opposite 
party have acted upon it.

9
28. 
It is also material that the decree in favour of the 
respondent/decree-holder has attained finality and the 
execution proceedings are meant only to enforce the 
same. The decree-holder cannot be deprived of the fruits 
of the decree by technical pleas raised by persons who 
themselves voluntarily stepped into the execution 
proceedings 
and 
assumed 
responsibility 
for 
the 
judgment-debtor. 
29. 
The appellants, being real brothers of the 
judgment-debtor, were fully conscious of the nature of 
the proceedings and the liability which they were 
undertaking. They cannot now be heard to say that they 
should be absolved from an obligation solemnly 
undertaken before a Court of law. 
30. 
The conduct of the judgment-debtor is also 
relevant. The record shows that he had earlier moved an 
application seeking permission to pay the decretal 
amount in instalments, which was dismissed up to this 
Court. The present attempt by the sureties to withdraw 
their bonds appears to be yet another effort to delay 
execution and frustrate the lawful claim of the decree-
holder. 
31. 
The learned Executing Court rightly observed 
that the appellants cannot be permitted to escape from the 
liability arising out of their own surety bonds. The 
impugned order does not suffer from any illegality,


# Page 10

10
misreading or non-reading of the record. Rather, the 
learned Executing Court has exercised jurisdiction in 
accordance with law and has protected the sanctity of the 
undertaking furnished before it. 
32. 
The conclusion is inescapable. The appellants 
voluntarily furnished surety bonds, expressly undertook 
to satisfy the decretal amount in the event of default by 
the judgment-debtor, and bound themselves before a 
Court of law. Neither the Contract Act, 1872 nor the 
Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 confers upon them a right 
to unilaterally withdraw from such undertaking after it 
has been accepted and acted upon. A judicial surety 
furnished for satisfaction of a decree stands on a 
fundamentally different footing from a continuing 
commercial guarantee and cannot be revoked unilaterally 
after acceptance by the Court. 
33. 
Consequently, this appeal, being devoid of merit, 
is dismissed. The order dated 02.11.2023 passed by the 
learned Executing Court is maintained.
