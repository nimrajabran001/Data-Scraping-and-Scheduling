11. 
In contrast to the Consumer Act, the HCC Act 
contains an express overriding clause. The said Act is 
not merely a subsequent enactment, but is also a 
special statute specifically designed to regulate 
healthcare establishments and to deal with matters 
relating to healthcare services, medical negligence, 
criminal negligence, malpractice, maladministration, 
quackery and allied subjects. Thus, while the 
Consumer Act provides a general additional remedy in 
respect of consumer goods and services, the HCC Act 
creates a specialized statutory regime for healthcare-
related complaints. Applying the principle generalia 
specialibus non derogant, matters falling within this 
specialized healthcare framework are to be dealt with 
by the forum created thereunder. Moreover, where two 
statutes appear to operate in an overlapping field, 
preference is to be accorded to the statute containing a 
non-obstante clause, as such clause reflects the express 
legislative intent to give overriding effect to that

HCC Act is reproduced below: 
33. Overriding effect. ----Notwithstanding anything 
to the contrary contained in any other law, the 
provisions of this Act shall have an overriding 
effect and the provisions of any such law to the 
extent of inconsistency to this Act shall cease to 
have effect." 
A bare reading of the aforesaid provision shows that,

to the extent of inconsistency. Similarly, the settled 
principle of statutory interpretation is that a special 
law prevails over a general law, and where two special 
laws conflict, the later enactment prevails over the 
earlier one.3
